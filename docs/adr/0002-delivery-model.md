# 0002 — Asymmetric delivery model: in-house assembly + coordinated machining network

Status: accepted

GPW delivers its two capabilities through different models. **Assembly & Integration is in-house** — GPW's core capability and the focus of its capital investment. **CNC Machining is coordinated** through a vetted network of Monterrey machine shops: GPW owns the engineering (DFM), quality assurance, nearshoring logistics, and customer accountability, but not the machining equipment. The site presents the managed machining network **explicitly, as a strength** (scalable capacity, no single-shop bottleneck, GPW-owned quality outcome) — the Xometry/Fictiv posture — never as a broker or pass-through.

## Why

- Matches GPW's real pre-revenue operating reality: machining can start now with low capital via coordination ("comisiones"), while assembly is the CAPEX-backed core that justifies investment.
- Keeps the vertical-integration story intact and honest: machined through the managed network → assembled and tested in-house → one accountable supplier.
- Avoids the credibility failure of claiming owned machining equipment GPW does not have. Risk-averse OEM procurement treats a single fabricated capability claim as poisoning the entire site.

## Consequences

- The 16 draft CNC copy files (written as in-house: "our CNC milling capabilities", "in-process inspection on every run", owned tolerances) must be rewritten to the managed-partner angle before building.
- Copy must never state or imply GPW owns machines, machine counts, or a machining shop floor; it must convey ownership of DFM, QA, traceability, and delivery accountability.
- Assembly (in-house) copy may claim direct capability; machining copy claims a managed, quality-governed network.
- Positioning guardrail: "managed manufacturing partner", never "broker / marketplace / middleman".
