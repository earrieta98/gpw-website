## 2. Brand Voice System

### 2.1 Brand Personality

GPW's brand personality can be described in five words:

| Attribute | What It Means | What It Doesn't Mean |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Precise** | Specific numbers, exact processes, measurable claims | Pedantic, overly technical, academic |
| **Confident** | Direct statements, active voice, no hedging | Arrogant, boastful, dismissive of competitors |
| **Engineering-Driven** | Process-focused, standards-aware, technically fluent | Jargon-heavy, inaccessible to procurement/business buyers |
| **Approachable** | Warm professionalism, partnership language, collaborative tone | Casual, chatty, using slang or humor |
| **Results-Oriented** | Outcome-focused, measurable benefits, time/cost specifics | Vague promises, unsubstantiated superlatives |

### 2.2 Voice vs. Tone

**Voice** is constant — it's who GPW is. GPW always sounds precise, confident, and engineering-driven.

**Tone** is contextual — it adjusts based on the page type, audience, and industry.

| Context | Tone Adjustment |
|---------|----------------|
| Homepage / Hero sections | Bold, aspirational, high-level |
| Service pages | Technical, detailed, process-focused |
| Industry pages | Sector-native, compliance-aware, specific |
| Blog posts (educational) | Accessible, explanatory, thought-leader |
| Blog posts (trends) | Forward-looking, data-backed, analytical |
| Comparison pages | Balanced, fact-based, confident without aggressive |
| About pages | Personal, credible, story-driven |
| RFQ / Contact pages | Reassuring, action-oriented, friction-reducing |
| FAQ sections | Direct, concise, no-fluff answers |

### 2.3 The GPW Voice Spectrum

Think of GPW's voice on a spectrum between two extremes. GPW lives in the middle-right zone:

```
Too Casual ←————————————————————→ Too Corporate
"Hey, we build stuff!"     [GPW LIVES HERE]     "We leverage synergistic..."
                           ↓
              "Precision electromechanical assembly,
               engineered for your production timeline."
```

**GPW's sweet spot:** Professional enough to earn trust from VP-level decision-makers, technical enough to satisfy engineers, and clear enough that procurement directors understand the value without needing a glossary.

### 2.4 Brand Phrases — Power Words

These are GPW's signature phrases. Use them naturally throughout the site (not forced into every paragraph):

**Primary phrases (high frequency):**
- "Engineered for [outcome]"
- "Built to [deliver/scale/spec]"
- "Ready to scale"
- "Precision [assembly/manufacturing/integration]"
- "From [start] to [finish]" (e.g., "From BOM to boxed product")

**Secondary phrases (medium frequency):**

- "Two hours from the border" (geographic advantage)
- "Same timezone, same standards" (collaboration advantage)
- "One supplier, one accountability chain"

**Tertiary phrases (occasional use):**
- "Your nearshore partner, not just a vendor"
- "Multi-vertical expertise under one roof"
- "Where engineering meets production"

### 2.5 Vocabulary Guidelines

**Words to use freely:**
| Category | Words |
|----------|-------|
| **Capability** | Precision, rigorous, specification, integration, scalable, flexible, capable, expertise |
| **Relationship** | Partner, collaborate, dedicated, responsive, aligned, transparent |
| **Quality** | Traceability, compliance, certified, validated, tested, verified, zero-defect |
| **Speed** | Rapid, agile, accelerated, streamlined, efficient, responsive |
| **Location** | Nearshore, Monterrey, USMCA-compliant, cross-border, same-timezone |

**Words to avoid:**
| Word/Phrase | Why | Use Instead |
|-------------|-----|-------------|
| "Best" / "world-class" | Unsubstantiated superlative | Specific capability or metric |
| "Innovative" / "cutting-edge" | Overused tech buzzwords | Describe the actual innovation |
| "Solutions" (alone) | Meaningless without context | "Assembly services" or specific capability |
| "Leverage" / "utilize" | Corporate jargon | "Use" or "apply" |
| "Maybe" / "might" / "perhaps" | Undermines confidence | State it or don't |
| "Revolutionary" / "disruptive" | Hype language | Describe the actual advantage |
| "Cheap" / "budget" / "discount" | Undermines premium positioning | "Cost-competitive" or "cost-effective" |
| "Maquiladora" | Negative connotation | "Nearshore facility" or "Monterrey operation" |
| "Outsource" | Negative framing | "Nearshore" or "partner with" |
| "Coming soon" / "will be" | Future tense for capabilities | Present tense (see Section 3.2) |

## 3. Writing Rules

### 3.1 The Seven Laws of GPW Copy

These rules apply to every piece of content on the site — no exceptions.

**Law 1: Clarity Over Cleverness**
If a clever headline sacrifices clarity, rewrite it. The reader should never have to re-read a sentence to understand it.

- ❌ "Where precision meets the horizon of possibility"
- ✅ "Precision electromechanical assembly in Monterrey, Mexico"

**Law 2: Benefits Over Features**
Features describe what GPW does. Benefits describe what the customer gets. Lead with benefits, support with features.

- ❌ "We have 50,000 sq ft of production floor space" (feature)
- ✅ "Scale from prototype to 10,000+ units without changing partners — our facility handles it" (benefit → feature)

**Law 3: Specificity Over Vagueness**
Vague copy sounds like every other EMS website. Specific copy builds trust.

- ❌ "Fast turnaround times"
- ✅ "First article builds in 2-3 weeks. Production ramp in 4-6 weeks."

**Law 4: Active Voice Over Passive Voice**
Active voice is more direct, confident, and readable.

- ❌ "Assemblies are tested by our quality team before shipment"
- ✅ "Our quality team tests every assembly before shipment"

**Law 5: One Idea Per Paragraph**
Each paragraph should advance one argument or present one concept. If a paragraph tries to cover two ideas, split it.

**Law 6: Customer Language Over Company Language**
Write the way OEM procurement teams talk, not the way an internal marketing team writes.

- ❌ "GPW leverages its robust operational infrastructure to facilitate seamless assembly workflows"
- ✅ "We take your BOM, build the assembly, test it, and ship it. On time."

**Law 7: Show, Don't Claim**
Instead of claiming a quality, demonstrate it through specifics.

- ❌ "We have an exceptional quality program"
- ✅ "Every unit goes through 47-point functional testing, thermal validation, and visual inspection before it leaves our floor"

### 3.2 Tense and Confidence Rules

**Present tense for all capabilities.** GPW writes as if it is already operating and actively delivering for customers. No future tense, no conditional language, no "coming soon."

- ❌ "GPW will offer box build assembly services"
- ✅ "GPW delivers box build assembly — mechanical, electrical, cabling, and testing — under one roof"

- ❌ "We plan to serve the aerospace industry"
- ✅ "Our aerospace sub-assembly process maintains full traceability from incoming inspection through final test"

**Exception — Certifications only:** Because certifications are third-party verifiable, use "pursuing" or "in progress" language for certifications not yet obtained. This is the *only* exception to the present-tense rule.

- ✅ "Pursuing ISO 13485 certification for medical device assembly"
- ✅ "IPC-A-610 and IPC-A-620 certification in progress"
- ✅ "AS9100 certification pathway underway for aerospace production"

**Why this exception exists:** A customer can verify certification status with a registrar. Claiming a certification you don't hold is a material misrepresentation. Claiming you build aerospace sub-assemblies (when you have the capability but not yet a customer) is a statement of operational readiness, not a verifiable credential.

### 3.3 Sentence Structure Guidelines

**Sentence length:** Vary between 8 and 25 words. Aim for an average of 15 words per sentence. Short sentences create punch. Longer sentences create flow. Alternate between them.

**Paragraph length:** 2-3 sentences maximum for web copy. One-sentence paragraphs are acceptable for emphasis.

**Reading level:** Target Grade 10-12 reading level. Technical vocabulary is fine (our audience knows the terms), but sentence structure should be simple and direct.

**Punctuation:**
- Use periods. Short sentences are powerful.
- Use dashes — sparingly — for parenthetical information.
- Use colons to introduce lists or explanations.
- Avoid semicolons in web copy (they slow reading).
- Never use exclamation points in body copy. Ever.
- Question marks are allowed in headlines, subheads, and FAQ sections.

### 3.4 Numbers and Data

**Use digits, not words** for any number that supports a claim:
- ✅ "2 hours from the U.S. border"
- ✅ "40-60% cost savings vs. domestic assembly"
- ✅ "48-hour RFQ response time"

**Be specific when possible:**
- ❌ "Significant cost savings"
- ✅ "40-60% cost savings vs. domestic U.S. assembly"

**Use ranges when exact numbers aren't available:**
- ✅ "First article builds in 2-3 weeks"
- ✅ "Production capacity for 500-10,000+ units per month"

**Always provide context for numbers:**
- ❌ "150 miles from the border" (so what?)
- ✅ "150 miles from the U.S. border — 2 hours by road, same-day shipping to Texas"

### 3.5 Formatting for Web

**Headers (H2s):** Should answer a question or state a benefit. Use them every 200-300 words.
- ❌ "Our Capabilities"
- ✅ "What We Assemble — And How We Deliver It"

**Bullet points:** Use for lists of 3+ items. Each bullet should be 1-2 sentences. Start each bullet with a bold keyword or action verb.

**Bold text:** Use to highlight key terms, numbers, or phrases within paragraphs. Don't bold entire sentences.

**Internal links:** 2-3 per page minimum. Use descriptive anchor text, not "click here."
- ❌ "Click here to learn more"
- ✅ "Explore our box build assembly process"

### 4.6 Comparison Page Template

**Word count:** 2,500-3,000 words
**Primary purpose:** Capture BOFU search traffic, position GPW against categories (not named competitors)
**Primary CTA:** Request Quote

```
SECTION 1: Introduction (150-200 words)
├── Frame the comparison: What two approaches are being compared?
├── Why this comparison matters to OEMs making sourcing decisions
└── Promise: "Here's what you need to know to make the right choice"

SECTION 2: Comparison Table (visual, 200-300 words of supporting context)
├── 8-12 criteria compared side by side
├── Categories: Cost, Speed, Quality, Flexibility, Risk, Proximity, etc.
├── Honest assessment: Acknowledge trade-offs
└── Table format: Easy to scan

SECTION 3: Deep Dive — Approach A (400-500 words)
├── What this approach offers
├── Where it works best
├── Limitations and trade-offs
└── Who it's ideal for

SECTION 4: Deep Dive — Approach B (400-500 words)
├── Same structure as Section 3
├── Position GPW's approach as "Approach B" when applicable
└── Specificity: concrete advantages, not abstract claims

SECTION 5: Decision Framework (300-400 words)
├── "Choose [Approach A] if..." — 3-4 criteria
├── "Choose [Approach B] if..." — 3-4 criteria
└── Positioning: Help the reader self-select into GPW's ideal customer profile

SECTION 6: FAQ (200-300 words)
├── 3-5 comparison-specific questions
└── Schema markup

SECTION 7: CTA (100-150 words)
├── "Ready to explore [Approach B]?"
├── Primary CTA: Request a Quote
└── Secondary CTA: Related industry or service page
```

**Writing notes:**
- Never name specific competitors on comparison pages
- Compare *categories of approach* (nearshore vs. offshore, mid-market vs. mega-EMS, agile vs. traditional)
- Be balanced and honest — acknowledge the strengths of the other approach
- The reader should feel informed, not manipulated
- GPW's advantage should emerge naturally from the comparison criteria

## 5. Industry Tone Adjustments

While GPW's voice remains consistent, the tone shifts slightly across industries. These adjustments help each industry page feel written by someone who understands that vertical.

|----------|-----------|-----------------|------------------|----------------|---------------|
| AI & Server Rack | Medium-High | Very High | Medium | Very High | Medium |
| Industrial Equipment | Medium | High | Low | Medium | High |

### 5.2 Industry-Specific Tone Notes

**AI & Server Rack Assembly**
- Lead with scale and speed — hyperscalers care about deployment velocity
- Technical specifics: thermal management, cable density, power distribution
- Use OEM-native language: "rack-level integration," "hot-swap compatibility," "burn-in testing"
- This is GPW's premium focus page — it can be more detailed than others
- Balance: ~40% of overall site messaging, but don't let it dominate every page

**Industrial Equipment**
- Practical, results-driven tone
- Emphasize cost-effectiveness and reliability over cutting-edge tech
- Use straightforward language: "control panel assembly," "sub-assembly manufacturing"
- Audience: Operations directors who value predictability

**Telecom Hardware**
- Standards-aware: reference telecom-specific certifications and requirements
- Network reliability language: "system uptime," "mission-critical integration"
- Balance between technical depth and procurement accessibility

**Medical Devices & Equipment**
- Most compliance-focused tone on the site
- Lead with regulatory awareness: "Pursuing ISO 13485," "controlled assembly environment"
- Never overstate certification status — this is legally sensitive
- Use: "controlled specifications," "documented traceability," "validated processes"
- Audience: Quality and regulatory affairs professionals alongside procurement

**Automotive Sub-assemblies**
- Production-volume language: "fixtures," "integration," "sub-assembly"
- Quality standards: IATF 16949 awareness, automotive supply chain terminology
- Position GPW for mid-complexity work, not heavy Tier 1 manufacturing
- Emphasis on wire harness and fixture assembly expertise

**Energy Sector**
- Reliability and durability language
- Standards awareness: UL, CSA, explosion-proof requirements
- Long product lifecycle perspective
- "Equipment cabinets," "power system integration," "field-deployable assemblies"

**Aerospace Sub-assemblies**
- Most formal tone on the site
- Traceability is the #1 topic: "lot tracking," "full material traceability," "serialized assembly"
- AS9100 awareness (pursuing)
- Conservative claims — aerospace buyers are the most skeptical audience
- No hyperbole, no marketing language — let the process speak

**Appliances & White Goods**
- Most volume-oriented, cost-focused tone
- Emphasize scale, efficiency, and speed-to-market
- Simpler technical language — the audience is more production/procurement focused
- "Consumer-grade durability," "assembly at scale," "cost-per-unit optimization"

## 6. CTA Writing Framework

### 6.1 CTA Hierarchy

GPW uses a three-tier CTA system across the site:

| Tier | Purpose | Button Color | Example |
|------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Primary** | Drive RFQ submissions | Coral #ED835E | "Request a Quote" |
| **Secondary** | Deeper engagement | Teal #23555A | "Download Guide" / "Schedule Tour" |
| **Tertiary** | Content discovery | Text link (no button) | "Explore our cable harness assembly process →" |

### 6.2 CTA Copy Formula

**Formula:** [Action Verb] + [What They Get] + [Qualifier if needed]

**Strong CTAs:**
- "Request a Quote" (primary, sitewide)
- "Request a Quote for [Industry] Assembly" (industry-specific)
- "Explore Our [Service] Process" (service discovery)
- "Download the Nearshoring Checklist" (lead magnet)
- "Schedule a Facility Tour" (relationship building)
- "Talk to Our Engineering Team" (technical credibility)
- "See How We Build [Product Type]" (process transparency)

**Weak CTAs (avoid):**
- "Submit" (too generic, feels like a form)
- "Learn More" (what will I learn?)
- "Click Here" (meaningless)
- "Get Started" (with what?)
- "Contact Us" (too passive — use "Talk to Our Team" instead)

### 6.3 CTA Placement by Page Type

| Page Type | Above Fold | Mid-Page | Bottom |
|-----------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Homepage | "Request a Quote" (Coral) | "Explore EMS Services" (Teal) | "Request a Quote" + "Download Brochure" |
| EMS Division Home | "Explore Our Services" (Coral) | "Request a Quote" (Teal) | "Request a Quote" + "Schedule Tour" |
| Service Page | "Request a Quote" (Coral) | "See How We Do It" (text link) | "Request a Quote" + "Explore [Related Service]" |
| Industry Page | "Request a Quote for [Industry]" (Coral) | "[Service] for [Industry]" (text link) | "Request a Quote" + "Explore [Related Industry]" |
| Blog Post | None (don't sell immediately) | "Explore [Related Service]" (text link) | "Request a Quote" (Coral) |
| Comparison Page | None | "See the Difference" (text link) | "Request a Quote" (Coral) |
| About Page | None | "Schedule a Facility Tour" (Teal) | "Request a Quote" (Coral) |

### 6.4 CTA Context Copy

Every CTA button should be accompanied by 1-2 lines of supporting context that reduces anxiety:

**For RFQ CTAs:**
- "No commitment required. We respond within 24 hours with a detailed assessment."
- "Tell us about your project. Our engineering team reviews every submission personally."

**For Tour CTAs:**
- "See our production floor, meet the team, and discuss your project on-site."
- "We're in Monterrey — a 2-hour flight from Austin, Dallas, or Phoenix."

**For Download CTAs:**
- "Free, no email required. See our full capabilities at a glance."
- "A 10-page overview of our services, industries, and quality approach."

## 7. Do/Don't Reference Library

### 7.1 Headlines

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| "Welcome to GPW Solutions" | "Precision Assembly. Nearshore Speed. One Partner." | Headlines should communicate value, not greet visitors |
| "Your Trusted Manufacturing Partner" | "Electromechanical Assembly Engineered for U.S. OEMs" | Be specific about what you do and for whom |
| "World-Class Quality" | "47-Point Functional Testing on Every Unit" | Show quality through specifics, don't claim it |
| "The Future of Manufacturing" | "Assembly in Monterrey. Delivered to Your Dock in Days, Not Months." | Ground the message in tangible benefits |

### 7.2 Body Copy

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| "GPW leverages its robust infrastructure to facilitate seamless manufacturing operations" | "We take your BOM, assemble the product, test it, and ship it. On time." | Customer language, not corporate jargon |
| "We are committed to delivering the highest quality products" | "Every assembly passes our end-of-line testing protocol — functional, thermal, and visual — before it ships" | Show the quality process, don't just commit to it |
| "Our state-of-the-art facility is equipped with cutting-edge technology" | "Our Monterrey facility runs 6 dedicated assembly lines with capacity for 500-10,000+ units per month" | Specifics build more trust than adjectives |
| "GPW is proud to announce that we will soon be offering aerospace assembly services" | "Our aerospace sub-assembly process maintains full traceability from incoming inspection through final test" | Present tense, operational language |

### 7.3 Certification Language

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| "GPW is ISO 13485 certified" | "Pursuing ISO 13485 certification for medical device assembly" | Don't claim certifications you don't have |
| "We meet AS9100 standards" | "AS9100 certification pathway underway for aerospace production" | "Meeting standards" implies certification |
| "IPC certified facility" | "IPC-A-610 and IPC-A-620 certification in progress. Our assembly processes are designed to meet IPC workmanship standards." | Separate process capability from formal certification |
| "We will pursue ISO 13485 next year" | "Pursuing ISO 13485 certification for medical device assembly" | No future tense — even "pursuing" should be present |

### 7.4 Competitive Positioning

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| "Unlike Jabil, we actually care about mid-market clients" | "Right-sized for Tier 1 and Tier 2 OEMs — dedicated attention without the bureaucracy of mega-EMS operations" | Never name competitors; position by category |
| "We're better than offshore manufacturers" | "Nearshore assembly from Monterrey reduces lead times by 40-60% compared to typical Asian sourcing — with same-timezone collaboration" | Be specific about the advantage, not subjective about superiority |
| "Don't choose a big EMS — choose us" | "When you need a partner who answers the phone, adjusts the schedule, and treats your 500-unit run like it matters — that's what a mid-market assembly partner delivers" | Show the benefit of the approach, not the weakness of the alternative |

### 7.5 Industry-Specific Do/Don'ts

**Medical:**
- ❌ "Our medical-grade facility ensures perfect compliance"
- ✅ "Our controlled assembly environment is designed for medical device requirements. ISO 13485 certification is in progress."

**Aerospace:**
- ❌ "We handle aerospace projects with the highest standards"
- ✅ "Full lot traceability, serialized assembly records, and documented material chain of custody — designed to meet aerospace requirements. AS9100 certification pathway underway."

**AI & Server Rack:**
- ❌ "We build the world's best servers"
- ✅ "Rack-level integration for hyperscaler and enterprise OEMs — thermal management, cable routing, power distribution, and burn-in testing under one program"

## 8. Pre-Publish Quality Checklist

Run through this checklist before submitting any page copy for review.

### 8.1 Voice & Tone

- [ ] Does it sound like GPW? Read it aloud — does it match the brand personality?
- [ ] Is the tone appropriate for this page type? (See Section 2.2)
- [ ] If it's an industry page, does the tone match that industry's adjustments? (See Section 5)
- [ ] Are brand power phrases used naturally (not forced)?
- [ ] Are "avoid" words absent from the copy?

### 8.2 Writing Quality

- [ ] Active voice throughout (minimal passive constructions)?
- [ ] Sentences average 15 words or fewer?
- [ ] Paragraphs are 2-3 sentences max?
- [ ] No exclamation points in body copy?
- [ ] Numbers use digits, not words?
- [ ] All claims are specific and substantiated?
- [ ] No "coming soon," "will be," or future tense for capabilities?
- [ ] Certifications use "pursuing" / "in progress" language where applicable?

### 8.3 Structure & SEO

- [ ] H1 includes primary keyword naturally?
- [ ] H2s answer search questions or state benefits?
- [ ] 3-5 H2s per page?
- [ ] Title tag: 50-60 characters with keyword + brand?
- [ ] Meta description: 155-160 characters with CTA language?
- [ ] Internal links: 2-3 minimum with descriptive anchor text?
- [ ] FAQ section with 5-7 questions (service/industry pages)?
- [ ] Definition block present (service/industry pages)?
- [ ] Word count within target range?

### 8.4 CTAs

- [ ] Primary CTA is present and visible?
- [ ] CTA placement follows the framework (above fold, mid-page, bottom)?
- [ ] CTA copy uses action verb + what they get?
- [ ] CTA has context copy (reassurance line)?
- [ ] Correct button colors (Coral for primary, Teal for secondary)?

### 8.5 Industry Balance

- [ ] AI & Server Rack content stays within ~40% of overall site messaging?
- [ ] Other 7 industries receive equitable depth and attention?
- [ ] Cross-industry internal links are present?
- [ ] No single industry dominates the corporate home page?

### 8.6 AI SEO Compatibility

- [ ] Definition blocks are self-contained and quotable?
- [ ] Answer blocks directly address common queries?
- [ ] Comparison tables use consistent formatting?
- [ ] FAQ answers are concise (40-60 words)?
- [ ] Page has schema markup plan (from AI SEO strategy)?

## 9. Quick Reference Cards

### 9.1 Before You Write — 5-Minute Prep

1. **Read the page brief** from Content Strategy Section 6
2. **Identify the primary persona** for this page (VP Supply Chain? Engineering Lead? Procurement Director?)
3. **Check the keyword** this page targets (from Topic Cluster mapping)
4. **Open this guide** to the relevant page template (Section 4)
5. **Check industry tone** if writing an industry page (Section 5)

### 9.2 While You Write — Keep Visible

- Voice: Precise, confident, engineering-driven, approachable, results-oriented
- Tense: Present tense for capabilities. "Pursuing" for unearned certifications only.
- Structure: Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences). Varied sentence length. H2 every 200-300 words.
- CTA: Coral for primary (RFQ). Teal for secondary. Text link for tertiary.
- Balance: 40% AI/servers, 60% other industries across the site.

### 9.3 After You Write — Review Pass

1. **Read aloud.** Does it sound like GPW or like generic marketing?
2. **Check the checklist** (Section 8).
3. **Verify links.** All internal links point to correct pages.
4. **Count words.** Within target range?
5. **Test the CTA.** Would you click it?
