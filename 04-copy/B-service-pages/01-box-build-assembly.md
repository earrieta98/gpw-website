## SECTION 2: What Is Box Build Assembly?

**Section Headline:**
What Is Box Build Assembly?

**Copy:**

Box build assembly is the process of integrating mechanical, electrical, and electromechanical components into a finished enclosure or housing. It takes individual parts — PCBAs, connectors, power supplies, fans, cables, brackets, and fasteners — and builds them into a complete product that is tested, labeled, and ready for shipment. Box build is distinct from PCBA fabrication: while a board house produces circuit boards, a box build facility takes those boards and all other components and assembles the final product.

**Key characteristics:**
- Combines mechanical sub-assemblies with electrical wiring and component integration
- Includes cable harness routing, connector installation, and power distribution
- Incorporates firmware loading, configuration, and functional testing
- Covers cosmetic inspection, labeling, and final packaging
- Requires documented processes, controlled workstations, and trained technicians

**Example:** A telecom equipment OEM needs a finished cabinet assembled from a sheet metal enclosure, 3 PCBAs, a power supply, 12 cable harnesses, and 40+ connectors. GPW can source every component through its own supplier network, procure through the customer's approved vendors, or receive customer-furnished materials — whichever model fits the program. GPW mounts the boards, routes the cables, installs the power supply, loads firmware, runs a 47-point functional test, applies labels, and ships a finished unit — packaged and palletized to the customer's specifications.

**Why this matters for OEMs:** Box build consolidates what would otherwise require 3-4 separate suppliers — a mechanical assembler, a cable shop, a test house, and a packaging provider — into a single program. One purchase order. One accountability chain. One quality system from receiving dock to shipping dock.

When those 3-4 suppliers are coordinated separately, every handoff introduces risk: damaged components in transit, miscommunication on revision levels, finger-pointing when a unit fails at the customer's dock. A single-source box build program eliminates those handoffs. GPW receives your components, assembles the product, tests it, packages it, and ships it — with one team responsible for the result.

## SECTION 3: GPW's Box Build Process

**Section Headline:**
How Does GPW Execute a Box Build Program?

**Copy:**

Every box build program at GPW follows a structured, repeatable process. This is not a general assembly floor where technicians improvise builds from drawings. Every step is documented, every station is configured for the specific product, and every quality gate is defined before the first unit enters the line.

### Step 1: Program Launch and DFM Review

When a customer submits an RFQ with a bill of materials, GPW's engineering team performs a full design-for-manufacturing (DFM) review before quoting. This review evaluates assembly sequence optimization, component accessibility, cable routing feasibility, test point locations, and potential risks.

The DFM review produces a detailed process plan: step-by-step assembly instructions, station assignments, fixture requirements, tool lists, and quality gate definitions. If GPW's engineers identify design improvements that reduce assembly time or improve reliability, those recommendations go back to the customer before production begins.

GPW's DFM review is not a formality. It is a working session where GPW's engineers flag potential improvements — assembly sequence changes that reduce cycle time, component placement adjustments that improve test access, or cable routing modifications that eliminate failure points. When improvements are identified, those recommendations go back to the customer for approval before production begins.

**Outcome:** A production-ready process plan with no ambiguity about how each unit will be built.

### Step 2: First Article Build and Validation

Before volume production begins, GPW builds and validates first article units. The first article build follows the process plan exactly as documented — no shortcuts, no workarounds. This build validates assembly sequence, fixture fit, cable routing paths, test protocol execution, and cycle time targets.

GPW documents every observation from the first article build: steps that took longer than planned, clearance issues, cable management improvements, and test protocol refinements. These observations feed back into the process plan as revisions before production ramp.

The first article build is where assumptions become facts. Estimated cycle times become measured cycle times. Cable routing paths that looked feasible on paper are validated physically. Test protocols are executed against real hardware, not simulations. GPW does not move to production until the first article results confirm the process plan is ready.

**Outcome:** A validated build process with documented cycle times and a first article inspection (FAI) report.

### Step 3: Production Assembly

Production assembly follows the validated process plan across dedicated workstations. Each station handles a defined scope — mechanical mounting, electrical integration, cable routing, or sub-assembly preparation. Operators work from visual work instructions displayed at the station, with each step requiring sign-off before the unit moves forward.

GPW manages component flow through a controlled material system. The receiving team inspects and kits every part before it reaches the assembly line. BOM-level traceability tracks every component from receiving through final shipment — including lot numbers, date codes, and supplier certifications.

This controlled material and assembly system eliminates two of the most common failure modes in contract assembly: wrong components reaching the line, and operators deviating from the documented process. Every unit is built the same way, every time.

**Outcome:** Assembled units that match the validated process plan, with full component traceability.

### Step 4: Testing, Inspection, and Quality Gates

Testing at GPW is not a single end-of-line checkpoint. GPW integrates quality gates throughout the assembly process:

- **Incoming inspection:** Components verified against the approved vendor list and incoming quality standards
- **In-process gates:** Visual inspection and continuity checks at critical assembly stations
- **Functional testing:** End-of-line testing with documented pass/fail criteria specific to each product
- **Burn-in (when required):** Extended operational testing under load to identify early-life failures
- **Hi-Pot testing:** High-potential voltage testing for electrical safety compliance
- **Final inspection:** Cosmetic review, label verification, and packaging audit

Each test produces documented results tied to the unit's serial number. If a unit fails any gate, technicians route it to a dedicated rework station — never pushing it through the line. The team documents the failure, identifies the root cause, and feeds corrective actions back into the process to prevent recurrence.

GPW's approach to testing reflects a core belief: catching a defect at station 3 costs a fraction of catching it at end-of-line — and orders of magnitude less than catching it at the customer's dock. Integrated testing is not just a quality measure. It is a cost control measure.

**Outcome:** Every shipped unit has a documented test record proving it meets specification.

### Step 5: Packaging, Labeling, and Shipment

The last step in GPW's box build process covers final cosmetic inspection, customer-specified labeling (barcodes, serial numbers, regulatory marks), and packaging. GPW packages products to customer specifications — including custom foam inserts, anti-static packaging, palletization patterns, and shipping documentation.

For programs requiring direct-to-customer shipment, GPW coordinates logistics from the Monterrey facility to U.S. destinations. USMCA-compliant documentation ensures duty-free border crossing for qualifying products.

GPW supports multiple packaging configurations: standard corrugated with foam inserts, ESD-safe packaging for sensitive electronics, multi-unit palletization for volume shipments, and customer-branded retail packaging for consumer-facing products. The packaging specification is documented as part of the process plan and validated during the first article build — just like every other step.

**Outcome:** Finished products arrive at your dock ready for distribution — no repackaging, no relabeling, no additional handling.
