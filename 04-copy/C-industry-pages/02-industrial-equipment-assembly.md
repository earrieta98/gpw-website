## SECTION 2: What Is Industrial Equipment Assembly?

**Section Headline:**
What Is Industrial Equipment Assembly?

**Copy:**

Industrial equipment assembly is the process of integrating mechanical, electrical, and electromechanical components into finished industrial products or sub-assemblies — control panels, motor-driven machinery, instrumentation enclosures, operator interfaces, and system-level equipment builds. It takes individual parts — circuit boards, contactors, relays, wiring, switches, sensors, enclosures, and structural components — and builds them into a tested, documented, production-ready unit. For OEMs that design industrial equipment, contract assembly transfers the build process to a specialized partner so the OEM can focus on engineering and market development.

## SECTION 4: GPW's Capabilities for Industrial Equipment Assembly

**Section Headline:**
What GPW Builds — Industrial Controls, Sub-Assemblies, and Complete Equipment

**Copy:**

GPW assembles industrial equipment across a range of product types and complexity levels. Every program starts with a documented process plan and ends with a tested, traceable product ready to ship.

### Control Panel and Enclosure Assembly

Control panels are among the most common industrial assembly programs at GPW. A typical control panel integrates a backplate with DIN rail-mounted components — PLCs, motor starters, contactors, relays, terminal blocks, circuit breakers, and power supplies — wired according to the OEM's electrical schematic.

GPW's control panel assembly process covers component mounting, wire routing and termination, labeling, point-to-point continuity verification, and functional testing against the customer's test specification. Panels are assembled inside enclosures rated for the installation environment — NEMA-rated or IP-rated housings that GPW sources through the customer's approved vendor list or procures directly.

For OEMs producing multiple panel variants, GPW manages configuration control across variants — ensuring the correct BOM, schematic revision, and test protocol are applied to each build without manual intervention or guesswork.

### Machinery Sub-Assembly and Integration

Industrial machinery often ships as a system composed of multiple sub-assemblies — motor modules, drive systems, sensor arrays, operator stations, and structural frames. GPW assembles these sub-assemblies individually and integrates them into the final machine configuration when the program requires system-level assembly.

Mechanical integration includes torque-controlled fastening, alignment verification, and fit checks against customer tolerances. Electrical integration covers motor wiring, sensor connections, control cable routing, and power distribution. GPW's technicians work from visual work instructions at configured workstations — each step documented, each connection verified.

### Instrumentation and Sensor Integration

Industrial equipment increasingly depends on embedded sensors, transmitters, and instrumentation for process control and monitoring. GPW installs and validates instrumentation packages within larger assemblies — calibrating sensor connections, verifying signal integrity, and confirming that instrumentation outputs match the expected values defined in the customer's test protocol.

### Electromechanical Sub-Assemblies

Not every program requires a complete equipment build. GPW assembles electromechanical sub-assemblies — motor mounts with wiring harnesses, actuator assemblies with integrated controls, valve manifolds with pneumatic tubing, and bracket-mounted PCBAs with interconnect cables — that the OEM integrates into their final product at their own facility. These sub-assembly programs follow the same process discipline as full equipment builds: documented procedures, quality gates, and traceability at every step.

## SECTION 7: Quality and Compliance for Industrial Equipment Assembly

**Section Headline:**
Quality Systems Built for Industrial Reliability

**Copy:**

Industrial equipment operates in demanding environments — factory floors, outdoor installations, high-vibration applications, and temperature extremes. Assembly quality directly impacts equipment reliability in the field. GPW's quality system ensures every unit leaves the production floor ready for those conditions.

**Process documentation:** Every assembly step follows documented work instructions displayed at the workstation. Operators execute each step in sequence, with verification required before the unit advances to the next station.

**Electrical verification:** GPW performs point-to-point continuity checks on every wired assembly. For control panels, every wire termination is verified against the customer's wiring schematic — not just visually inspected, but electrically confirmed.

**Functional testing:** GPW tests every unit against the customer's functional test specification before shipment. For control panels, this includes power-up verification, PLC communication checks, I/O validation, and motor circuit testing where applicable.

**Traceability:** Every component and assembly step is traceable to the individual unit by serial number. GPW's records include component lot numbers, operator identification, test results, and date codes — available for customer audit at any time.

**Certifications and standards:**
- IPC-A-610 (acceptability of electronic assemblies) — workmanship standard for all electrical integration
- IPC-A-620 (cable and wire harness assemblies) — standard for wiring and termination work
- ISO 9001 quality management system — certification in progress
- UL 508A panel shop listing — in progress for industrial control panel assembly
- Customer-specific quality requirements accommodated through program-level quality plans

## SECTION 8: Frequently Asked Questions — Industrial Equipment Assembly

**Section Headline:**
FAQ: Industrial Equipment Assembly at GPW

**Q: What types of industrial equipment does GPW assemble?**

GPW assembles control panels, machinery sub-assemblies, instrumentation enclosures, motor-driven modules, complete equipment builds, and electromechanical sub-assemblies. Programs range from simple panel builds with 20 components to complex machinery systems integrating mechanical, electrical, and pneumatic systems. If it involves electromechanical integration into a finished or semi-finished industrial product, GPW can build it.

**Q: Does GPW handle custom tooling and fixtures for industrial programs?**

Yes. GPW designs and builds custom assembly fixtures, holding jigs, and test fixtures as part of the program launch process. Fixture design is based on the DFM review and optimized for repeatability and operator ergonomics. Fixture costs are included in the NRE (non-recurring engineering) phase and remain with the program.

**Q: What is GPW's capacity for industrial equipment assembly?**

GPW scales capacity to match program demand. Current production supports programs ranging from 25 units per month to 1,000+ units per month, depending on product complexity and labor content per unit. Staffing and production scheduling adjust to your demand cycle — whether you run 50 units during low season or 500 during peak.

**Q: Can GPW support rapid design changes during production?**

Yes. GPW uses a formal engineering change order (ECO) workflow. The engineering team assesses impact on assembly sequence, materials, fixtures, and test protocols, then updates documentation and implements changes with documented effectivity. Most changes require no production interruption — the transition is managed within normal production flow.

**Q: Does GPW source components or work with customer-furnished materials?**

Both. GPW procures components through customer-approved vendor lists or its own supplier network, and also receives and manages customer-furnished materials. Hybrid models — where GPW sources some components and the customer furnishes others — are common in industrial programs. Full BOM-level traceability applies regardless of sourcing model.

**Q: Is GPW pursuing UL 508A listing for industrial control panels?**

Yes. GPW is in the process of obtaining UL 508A listing for industrial control panel assembly. Current production already follows UL 508A standards and construction practices — including proper wire sizing, overcurrent protection, and enclosure ratings — in preparation for formal listing and third-party audit.

**Q: How are finished industrial assemblies shipped to the U.S.?**

Assemblies ship by truck from Monterrey, reaching Texas distribution points in 1-2 days. GPW manages custom packaging designed for each product's weight and fragility, handles export documentation, and provides USMCA-compliant paperwork for preferential tariff treatment on cross-border shipments.
