## SECTION: Why Machine Plastics

**Label:** When Plastic Beats Metal

**H2:** Why CNC Machine Plastics?

Engineering plastics replace metal in applications where you need lighter weight, chemical resistance, electrical insulation, or biocompatibility — without sacrificing structural performance.

CNC machining is the right process for plastic parts when:

- **Quantity is low to medium** — under 500 parts, machining is almost always cheaper than injection molding because there is no tooling investment
- **Tolerances are tight** — machining holds ±0.002" on most plastics, ±0.001" on PEEK and Delrin. Injection molding typically holds ±0.005" without secondary machining
- **Material is expensive** — PEEK costs $50–150 per pound. Machining from rod or plate wastes less material than molding with its sprues and runners
- **Design is still evolving** — no tooling means no tooling changes. Iterate your design freely between prototypes

**CNC machining vs injection molding — when to use which:**

| Factor | CNC Machining | Injection Molding |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Quantity | 1–500 parts | 5,000+ parts |
| Tooling cost | $0 | $5,000–$50,000+ |
| Lead time | 2–4 weeks | 8–16 weeks (including tooling) |
| Tolerance | ±0.001"–0.002" | ±0.005" typical |
| Design changes | Free — update the CAD file | $1,000–$10,000+ per tool modification |
| Per-piece cost | Higher | Lower at volume |
| Materials | Any machinable plastic | Must flow for injection |

**500–5,000 parts?** It depends on geometry, material, and tolerance. Upload your part and we will recommend the right process.

## SECTION: High-Performance

**H2:** High-Performance Engineering Plastics

These materials compete with metals. They cost more, machine slower, and require experienced operators — but they deliver properties that no metal can match in certain applications.

### PEEK (Polyether Ether Ketone)

The highest-performing machinable plastic. If you could only have one engineering plastic, this is the one.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 14,500 PSI (100 MPa) |
| Continuous Service Temp | 480°F (250°C) |
| Chemical Resistance | Resists nearly all chemicals except concentrated sulfuric acid |
| Biocompatibility | Yes — per ISO 10993 |
| Machinability | Moderate — requires sharp carbide tooling, controlled feeds |
| Density | 0.047 lb/in³ (1.30 g/cm³) — 80% lighter than aluminum |
| Radiation | Radiolucent — invisible to X-ray |

**Grades available:**
- **Standard PEEK** — Unfilled, best for biocompatible and chemical applications
- **30% Glass-Filled PEEK** — Higher stiffness, better dimensional stability, reduced thermal expansion

**Best for:** Spinal implants, orthopedic bearing surfaces, semiconductor handling, oil & gas seals, aerospace bushings, chemical processing components. Any application that demands heat resistance, chemical resistance, and mechanical strength simultaneously.

**Cost note:** PEEK rod stock costs $50–150 per pound. Machine only what the application demands — our DFM review checks whether a lower-cost plastic meets your requirements.

**Industries:** Medical, aerospace, semiconductor, oil & gas, chemical processing

---

### Delrin / Acetal (POM)

The engineer's favorite plastic. Machines like butter, holds tight tolerances, and costs a fraction of PEEK.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 10,000 PSI (69 MPa) |
| Continuous Service Temp | 180°F (82°C) |
| Moisture Absorption | 0.2% (excellent dimensional stability) |
| Coefficient of Friction | Low — naturally self-lubricating |
| Machinability | Excellent — the best-machining plastic. Clean chips, no burrs. |
| FDA Compliant | Yes — food-contact grades available |

**Grades available:**
- **Delrin 150 (Standard)** — Best all-around. Use as the default.
- **Delrin AF (PTFE-filled)** — Lowest friction. For bearings and slides.
- **30% Glass-Filled Delrin** — Highest stiffness and dimensional stability.

**Best for:** Gears, bearings, bushings, rollers, guides, valve components, conveyor parts, food handling equipment. If your plastic part moves against another surface, Delrin is probably the right choice.

**Industries:** Industrial, food processing, automotive, consumer products, medical devices

---

### Nylon 6/6

Strong, tough, and abrasion-resistant. The plastic that takes a beating.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 12,400 PSI (85 MPa) |
| Impact Resistance | High — absorbs shock without fracturing |
| Abrasion Resistance | Excellent |
| Moisture Absorption | 2.5% — this is the tradeoff |
| Machinability | Good — can produce stringy chips. Glass-filled machines cleaner. |

**Grades available:**
- **Standard Nylon 6/6** — General purpose
- **30% Glass-Filled Nylon** — Higher stiffness, better dimensional stability, lower moisture absorption

**Best for:** Structural brackets, wear pads, cable guides, snap-fit housings, wear components, non-lubricated bearings.

**Critical note on moisture:** Nylon absorbs moisture from the air. A part machined to ±0.001" dry may grow 0.005" after weeks in a humid environment. For tight-tolerance nylon parts, specify glass-filled (0.9% absorption vs 2.5%) or condition the parts to equilibrium before final machining.

**Industries:** Automotive, industrial, consumer products

---

### PTFE / Teflon

The lowest-friction, most chemically inert solid material available.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Coefficient of Friction | 0.04 — lowest of any solid material |
| Temperature Range | -400°F to +500°F (-240°C to +260°C) |
| Chemical Resistance | Inert to virtually every chemical |
| Tensile Strength | 3,000 PSI (21 MPa) — low |
| Machinability | Challenging — soft, deforms under clamping pressure |

**Best for:** Seals, gaskets, bearings, insulators, valve seats, chemical-resistant liners, non-stick surfaces.

**Design note:** PTFE is soft and weak. It is not a structural material. Parts must be designed with generous wall thickness and supported properly during machining. Our DFM review flags PTFE-specific issues before quoting.

**Industries:** Chemical processing, medical, aerospace, food processing, semiconductor

## SECTION: General Purpose

**H2:** General-Purpose Plastics

These materials handle common applications at lower cost than the high-performance group. Easier to machine, widely available, and suitable for most non-extreme environments.

### Polycarbonate (PC)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 9,500 PSI |
| Impact Resistance | Virtually unbreakable — 250× stronger than glass |
| Optical Clarity | Transparent — 88% light transmission |
| Machinability | Good — use sharp tools, can crack if clamped too hard |

**Best for:** Sight glasses, light covers, protective shields, transparent housings, display windows, lenses.

**Polycarbonate vs Acrylic:** PC is 30× more impact-resistant but scratches easier and costs more. Acrylic has better optical clarity but shatters on impact. Use PC when it might get hit. Use acrylic when optics matter and impact is not a concern.

**Industries:** Medical, electronics, industrial, consumer

---

### ABS

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 6,000 PSI |
| Impact Resistance | Good |
| Machinability | Excellent — easy to machine, sand, and paint |
| Cost | Low |

**Best for:** Prototypes, non-structural housings, enclosures, consumer product mockups, functional prototypes for testing form and fit.

**Note:** ABS is a prototyping and low-stress material. For structural or precision applications, use Delrin, nylon, or PEEK.

**Industries:** Consumer products, prototyping, automotive interiors

---

### Acrylic (PMMA)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Optical Clarity | 92% light transmission — clearer than polycarbonate |
| UV Resistance | Excellent — does not yellow |
| Machinability | Good — brittle, chips easily, can crack |

**Best for:** Light pipes, optical lenses, display components, signage, decorative panels.

**Caution:** Acrylic shatters on impact. Do not use where parts may be dropped, hit, or subjected to vibration. Use polycarbonate instead.

---

### UHMW-PE (Ultra-High Molecular Weight Polyethylene)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Abrasion Resistance | Highest of any plastic |
| Impact Resistance | Excellent — even at low temperatures |
| Coefficient of Friction | Very low — self-lubricating |
| FDA Compliant | Yes |
| Machinability | Good — but soft, can deform under clamping |

**Best for:** Conveyor guides, wear strips, chute liners, starwheels, food processing components, bearings, dock bumpers.

**Industries:** Food processing, material handling, industrial, packaging

---

### PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Chemical Resistance | Excellent — acids, bases, salts |
| Flame Retardant | Self-extinguishing |
| Cost | Low |
| Machinability | Good |

**Best for:** Chemical tanks, fittings, pipe flanges, ductwork, electrical insulation, corrosion-resistant housings.

**Industries:** Chemical processing, water treatment, construction, electrical

## SECTION: Specialty

**H2:** Specialty / High-Temperature Plastics

### Ultem / PEI (Polyetherimide)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Continuous Service Temp | 340°F (170°C) |
| Flame Rating | UL94 V-0 (self-extinguishing without dripping) |
| Sterilizable | Yes — autoclave, gamma, EtO |
| Tensile Strength | 15,200 PSI |

**Best for:** Aerospace interior components (meets FAR 25.853 flammability), medical sterilizable housings, electrical connectors exposed to heat.

**PEEK vs Ultem:** PEEK handles higher temperatures (480°F vs 340°F) and is more chemically resistant. Ultem is lighter, easier to machine, and cheaper. If 340°F is sufficient, Ultem saves money.

**Industries:** Aerospace, medical, electronics

---

### Garolite G-10

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Type | Glass-epoxy laminate (fiberglass + epoxy resin) |
| Electrical Insulation | Excellent |
| Dimensional Stability | Excellent |
| Moisture Absorption | Very low |
| Machinability | Abrasive — requires carbide or diamond tooling. Produces fine dust. |

**Best for:** Electrical insulation boards, PCB test fixtures, jigs, structural insulators, washers, spacers.

**Note:** Garolite dust is abrasive and harmful. Machining requires proper dust extraction. Tool wear is high — factor carbide tooling into cost.

**Industries:** Electronics, industrial testing, electrical

---

### Torlon (PAI — Polyamide-Imide)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Continuous Service Temp | 500°F (260°C) |
| Tensile Strength | 21,000 PSI — highest of any unreinforced plastic |
| Wear Resistance | Exceeds many metals |
| Cost | Very high |

**Best for:** Extreme-environment bearings, seals, and structural components. Aerospace bushings, oil & gas downhole seals, semiconductor handling.

**Note:** Torlon is the last resort before metal. If no other plastic can handle your temperature, load, or wear requirements, Torlon can. But it costs 5–10× more than PEEK and machines slowly.

**Industries:** Aerospace, oil & gas, semiconductor

---

### Polypropylene (PP)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Chemical Resistance | Excellent |
| Fatigue Resistance | Excellent — integral living hinges |
| FDA Compliant | Yes |
| Machinability | Difficult — soft, flexible, hard to hold tolerances |

**Best for:** Chemical tanks, lab equipment, hinges, low-stress housings, containers.

**Note:** PP is soft and flexible — difficult to machine to tight tolerances. Specify ±0.005" minimum. For tighter tolerances in a chemical-resistant plastic, use Delrin or PEEK.

## SECTION: Machining Tips

**H2:** Machining Plastics — What Engineers Should Know

Plastics are not metals. They behave differently under cutting forces, heat, and clamping. These factors affect your tolerances, surface finish, and part cost:

**Thermal expansion** — Plastics expand 5–10× more than metals per degree of temperature change. A part machined to size at 70°F may not fit at 120°F. Design with thermal expansion in mind, or specify operating temperature on your drawing.

**Moisture absorption** — Nylon absorbs 2.5% moisture by weight. That moisture swells the part dimensionally. If your nylon part needs tight tolerances, specify glass-filled nylon (0.9% absorption) or condition the parts before final machining.

**Chip management** — Some plastics (nylon, UHMW) produce long stringy chips that wrap around tooling. Others (Delrin, PEEK) produce clean chips that clear easily. Chip behavior affects cycle time and surface finish.

**Clamping pressure** — Soft plastics (PTFE, UHMW, PP) deform under normal clamping force. Custom soft jaws or vacuum fixtures may be needed for precision work. Factor fixturing into cost.

**Tolerances** — ±0.002" is the realistic standard for most plastics. ±0.001" is achievable on PEEK and Delrin with proper fixturing. Do not specify ±0.0005" on a nylon part — the material itself cannot hold that precision due to moisture and thermal effects.

**Stress relief** — Aggressive machining creates internal stresses in plastic parts. These stresses can cause warping days or weeks after machining. For critical parts, we apply stress-relief annealing between roughing and finishing operations.

GPW accounts for all of these factors in our DFM review. If you specify a tolerance that the material cannot hold, we will tell you — and suggest an alternative.

## SECTION: FAQ

**H2:** Common Questions About CNC Machining Plastics

**Q: Which plastic machines the best?**
Delrin (Acetal). Clean chips, excellent surface finish, tight tolerances achievable, minimal tool wear. It is to plastics what brass 360 is to metals — the machinability benchmark.

**Q: What is the tightest tolerance achievable on a plastic part?**
±0.001" on PEEK and Delrin with proper fixturing and controlled environment. ±0.002" on most other engineering plastics. Anything tighter than ±0.002" should be discussed during DFM review — material properties are often the limiting factor, not the machine.

**Q: CNC machining vs injection molding — which should I choose?**
Under 500 parts: machine. Over 5,000 parts: mold. Between 500 and 5,000: depends on geometry, material, and tolerance. Upload your part and we will recommend.

**Q: Can you machine filled or reinforced plastics?**
Yes — glass-filled Delrin, glass-filled PEEK, glass-filled nylon, and carbon-filled variants. Filled plastics require carbide tooling and produce abrasive chips. Factor higher tooling cost into pricing.

**Q: Do you stock plastic rod and plate?**
We source materials from verified suppliers. Common materials (Delrin, nylon, polycarbonate, ABS) ship quickly. Specialty materials (PEEK, Torlon, medical-grade) may have longer lead times — we confirm availability with your quote.
