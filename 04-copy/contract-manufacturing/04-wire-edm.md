## SECTION: What Is Wire EDM?

**Label:** The Process

**Definition Block:**

**Wire EDM** (Electrical Discharge Machining) uses a thin brass wire — as small as 0.004" in diameter — to cut through conductive materials via controlled electrical sparks. The wire never touches the workpiece. There is no mechanical cutting force, no heat-affected zone, and no tool wear in the traditional sense.

**H2:** Why Wire EDM Exists

Conventional milling and turning use cutting tools that are softer than some of the materials they need to cut. Tool steel hardened to 60 HRC destroys end mills. Tungsten carbide breaks tooling on contact. Internal corners sharper than the tool radius are physically impossible.

Wire EDM solves all three problems. It cuts hardened steel as easily as aluminum. It produces internal corners as sharp as the wire radius — 0.002". And it does both with tolerances that conventional machining cannot match.

GPW's engineering team evaluates every project to determine whether EDM is the right process — or whether milling, turning, or a combination delivers the same result at lower cost. If milling works, we will not quote you for EDM.

**Multi-pass cutting:**
Wire EDM can make multiple passes over the same cut — each pass improves surface finish and dimensional accuracy. A single rough pass delivers Ra 0.8 µm and ±0.001" tolerance. Three or four passes bring the surface to Ra 0.2 µm and the tolerance to ±0.0001". More passes mean longer cycle time and higher cost — we recommend the right number of passes for your actual requirements.

## SECTION: When to Use Wire EDM

**H2:** When to Use Wire EDM — A Decision Guide

Wire EDM is the right process when any of these conditions apply:

**Material hardness above 45 HRC**
Hardened tool steels (D2, A2, H13, S7), tungsten carbide, and hardened stainless cannot be conventionally milled without destroying tooling. EDM cuts them without resistance — hardness is irrelevant.

**Internal corners sharper than 0.5mm radius**
End mills have a radius. They cannot cut a perfectly sharp internal corner. Wire EDM can — limited only by the wire radius (as small as 0.002").

**Thin slots narrower than 0.5mm**
EDM can cut slots as narrow as 0.005" — thinner than any end mill.

**Tolerances tighter than ±0.0005"**
When your tolerance band is measured in tenths of a thousandth, EDM is the process that delivers consistently.

**Complex 2D profiles through thick material**
Intricate contours cut through 4" of hardened steel with the same ease as 0.25" — wire EDM does not care about depth, only perimeter length.

**Fragile or thin-walled parts**
Zero cutting force means no deflection, no chatter, no distortion. Parts with walls as thin as 0.010" can be EDM-cut without damage.

## SECTION: Applications

**H2:** What We Cut with Wire EDM

**Dies and punches** — Progressive dies, blanking dies, forming dies, trim dies. Cut after hardening — the die is machined to final dimension in its hardened state.

**Mold components** — Injection mold cores, cavities, inserts, slides, lifters. Complex profiles in hardened H13 or S7.

**Tooling and fixtures** — Precision gauges, go/no-go fixtures, inspection tools, custom jigs. Dimensional stability of EDM-cut surfaces outperforms milled surfaces in hardened material.

**Aerospace components** — Turbine blade root forms (fir-tree profiles), spline profiles, engine bracket features in titanium and Inconel.

**Medical instruments** — Surgical blade profiles, micro-features on instrument tips, complex internal geometries in hardened stainless.

**Gears and splines** — Precision gear tooth profiles, spline gauges, gear inspection masters. EDM produces the geometry that generates the gear profile.

**Extrusion dies** — Complex cross-section profiles for aluminum and plastic extrusion.

## SECTION: EDM vs Milling

**H2:** Wire EDM vs CNC Milling — How to Choose

| Factor | Wire EDM | CNC Milling |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Material hardness | Any hardness — 60+ HRC no problem | Practical limit ~45 HRC |
| Tolerances | ±0.0001" achievable | ±0.0005" achievable |
| Internal corners | Sharp (wire radius = 0.002") | Radius of end mill (min ~0.015") |
| Surface finish | Ra 0.2 µm (multi-pass) | Ra 0.4 µm (finish pass) |
| Cutting speed | Slower (inches per hour) | Faster (inches per minute) |
| Cost per part | Higher (slower cycle time) | Lower for most geometries |
| 3D geometry | Limited — 2D profile through material | Full 3D capability |
| Part thickness | Unlimited (same speed regardless) | Limited by tool reach |
| Cutting force | Zero | Significant (can deflect thin walls) |
| Setup | Simpler (fewer fixtures) | More complex (multiple setups for complex parts) |

**Bottom line:** Use milling when you can. Use EDM when you must — hardened materials, ultra-tight tolerances, sharp corners, thin slots, or fragile geometries. Many precision programs combine both: mill the part in its annealed state, harden it, then EDM the critical features.

## SECTION: FAQ

**H2:** Common Questions About Wire EDM

**Q: Can you cut hardened steel?**
Yes — that is EDM's primary advantage. We cut tool steel at 60+ HRC, tungsten carbide, and any other conductive material regardless of hardness. The material does not resist the process because there is no mechanical contact.

**Q: What is the thinnest slot you can cut?**
As narrow as 0.005" (0.127mm) — determined by wire diameter plus the spark gap on each side. Standard wire produces slots around 0.010"–0.012".

**Q: How does wire EDM compare on cost to milling?**
EDM is slower than milling, so per-part cost is higher for geometries that milling can handle. But EDM eliminates the need for expensive carbide tooling in hardened materials, reduces secondary grinding, and often completes in fewer setups. For hardened parts, EDM is frequently cheaper total cost when you include tooling, grinding, and setup time that milling would require.

**Q: Can you cut tapered profiles?**
Yes — up to 30° taper. The upper and lower wire guides move independently, allowing the wire to cut at an angle through the workpiece. Common for extrusion dies and parts with draft angles.

**Q: Lead times?**
2–4 weeks depending on complexity, number of passes, and material. Expedite available for critical tooling and die repairs.
