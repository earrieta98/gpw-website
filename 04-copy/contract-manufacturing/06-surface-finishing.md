## SECTION: Why Finishing Matters

**Label:** Beyond the Machine

**H2:** Why Surface Finishing Matters

A machined part is not a finished part. Raw machined surfaces corrode, wear, and look unfinished. The right surface treatment adds:

- **Corrosion resistance** — parts that survive salt spray, chemicals, and outdoor exposure
- **Wear resistance** — surfaces that last thousands of cycles without degradation
- **Appearance** — consistent color, texture, and finish across every part in the batch
- **Conductivity or insulation** — electrical properties controlled by the coating
- **Compliance** — industry-specific requirements (MIL specs, FDA, RoHS)

GPW coordinates all finishing as part of your machining project. You send us a drawing, we deliver a complete part — machined, finished, inspected, and documented. No separate POs, no coordinating between vendors, no delays from handoff errors.

## SECTION: Surface Treatments

**H2:** Surface Treatments

### Anodizing Type II

**What it does:** Builds a controlled oxide layer on aluminum through an electrochemical process. The layer is integral to the metal — it does not peel, chip, or flake.

**Specs:**
- Thickness: 0.0002"–0.001" (5–25 µm)
- Colors: Clear, black, red, blue, gold, green, custom
- Hardness: 200–400 HV
- Spec: MIL-A-8625 / MIL-PRF-8625 Type II

**Best for:** Aluminum parts that need corrosion protection, cosmetic color, or both. Consumer products, medical housings, electronics enclosures, aerospace brackets.

**Note:** Type II anodize adds dimension. A 0.001" anodize layer means 0.0005" growth per side. Our DFM review accounts for this in critical dimensions.

---

### Anodizing Type III (Hard Coat)

**What it does:** Thicker, denser oxide layer than Type II. Significantly harder — comparable to case-hardened steel.

**Specs:**
- Thickness: 0.001"–0.003" (25–75 µm)
- Colors: Natural dark gray/bronze. Can be dyed black.
- Hardness: 400–700 HV
- Spec: MIL-A-8625 / MIL-PRF-8625 Type III

**Best for:** Aluminum parts under heavy wear, friction, or repeated contact. Pistons, slides, valve bodies, rollers, military equipment.

**Note:** Hard coat adds more dimension than Type II and affects surface finish. Specify pre-anodize dimensions on your drawing.

---

### Bead Blasting

**What it does:** Propels fine glass beads at the surface under pressure. Creates a uniform, non-directional matte texture that hides machining marks.

**Best for:** Cosmetic parts where a consistent satin finish is needed. Often used as a pre-treatment before anodizing — gives anodized parts a smooth, even appearance instead of showing tool paths.

**Note:** Does not add corrosion protection on its own. Combine with anodize or clear coat for protection.

---

### Tumbling / Vibratory Finishing

**What it does:** Parts tumble in a drum or vibrate in a bowl with abrasive media. Deburrs edges, breaks sharp corners, and smooths surfaces uniformly.

**Best for:** Batch deburring of small parts. Removes burrs and sharp edges from CNC machining and laser cutting without manual labor. Cost-effective for high quantities.

---

### Polishing

**What it does:** Progressive abrasive smoothing — from coarse to fine — to achieve a reflective or near-reflective surface.

**Levels:**
- **Brushed** — Uniform directional grain lines. Industrial aesthetic.
- **Satin** — Smooth with slight sheen. Semi-reflective.
- **Mirror** — Fully reflective. Requires multiple polishing stages.

**Best for:** Consumer-facing products, medical devices, optical components, food-contact surfaces.

---

### Electropolishing

**What it does:** Electrochemical process that removes a thin layer of surface material. Produces an ultra-smooth, bright finish and eliminates micro-crevices where bacteria can harbor.

**Surface finish:** Ra 0.1–0.4 µm achievable

**Best for:** Stainless steel parts for medical, pharmaceutical, food processing, and semiconductor applications. Required for many implant and fluid-contact surfaces.

**Note:** Electropolishing removes 0.0002"–0.001" of material per side. Specify pre-polish dimensions.

---

### Passivation

**What it does:** Chemical bath (nitric or citric acid) that removes free iron from stainless steel surfaces. The chromium-rich surface layer that remains resists corrosion.

**Spec:** ASTM A967 / AMS 2700

**Best for:** Every stainless steel part. Passivation is standard practice for medical, food, pharmaceutical, and chemical processing components. Laser-cut stainless edges particularly benefit — cutting introduces free iron that passivation removes.

## SECTION: Plating

**H2:** Plating

### Electroless Nickel Plating

**What it does:** Deposits a uniform nickel-phosphorus layer without electrical current. Coats complex geometries evenly — internal channels, blind holes, and threads all receive the same thickness.

**Specs:**
- Thickness: 0.0002"–0.002" (5–50 µm)
- Hardness: 45–70 HRC (after heat treatment)
- Corrosion resistance: 500+ hours salt spray (ASTM B117)

**Best for:** Parts with complex geometry that need uniform corrosion and wear protection. Hydraulic components, valve bodies, connectors.

---

### Zinc Plating

**What it does:** Thin sacrificial zinc layer on steel. The zinc corrodes before the steel does — protecting the base metal.

**Specs:**
- Thickness: 0.0002"–0.001"
- Chromate options: Clear (silver), yellow (gold), black
- Spec: ASTM B633

**Best for:** Low-cost corrosion protection for steel parts. Brackets, fasteners, hardware, non-cosmetic structural parts.

---

### Chrome Plating

**Hard chrome:**
- Thickness: 0.001"–0.010"
- Hardness: 68–72 HRC
- Best for: Wear surfaces — hydraulic cylinders, piston rods, rollers, tooling

**Decorative chrome:**
- Thickness: 0.00001"–0.00003" (over nickel base layer)
- Best for: Aesthetic finish — visible parts, consumer products

---

### Copper Plating

**What it does:** Deposits a copper layer, typically as a base layer before nickel and chrome in multi-layer plating. Also used for EMI/RFI shielding on enclosures.

**Best for:** Pre-plating base layer, EMI shielding, heat conductivity enhancement.

## SECTION: Coatings

**H2:** Coatings

### Powder Coating

**What it does:** Dry powder (polyester, epoxy, or hybrid) sprayed electrostatically and cured at 350–400°F. Creates a thick, durable, uniform coating.

**Specs:**
- Thickness: 0.002"–0.006" (50–150 µm)
- Colors: Full RAL and Pantone range
- Properties: Chip-resistant, UV-stable, scratch-resistant
- Outdoor durability: 5–10+ years depending on powder type

**Best for:** Any metal part that needs color and protection. Enclosures, brackets, frames, panels, consumer products. The most common finish for sheet metal fabrication.

---

### Wet Paint

**What it does:** Liquid spray paint — primer + topcoat. More flexible than powder coat for multi-color, small batches, and exact color matching.

**Best for:** Color matching to Pantone, small quantities where powder coating setup cost is not justified, multi-color parts, parts that cannot withstand 350°F curing temperature.

---

### Chem Film / Chromate Conversion (Alodine)

**What it does:** Chemical conversion coating on aluminum. Produces a thin, conductive, corrosion-resistant layer.

**Specs:**
- Thickness: 0.000001"–0.00004" (negligible dimensional change)
- Types: Type I (gold/iridescent) or Type II (clear)
- Spec: MIL-DTL-5541
- Conductivity: Maintains electrical contact — unlike anodize which insulates

**Best for:** Aluminum parts that need corrosion protection AND electrical conductivity. Aerospace grounding points, electrical enclosures, EMI shielding. Also used as a paint or primer adhesion promoter.

---

### Black Oxide

**What it does:** Chemical conversion coating on steel. Produces a dark black surface with minimal dimensional change.

**Specs:**
- Thickness: 0.000005" (essentially zero dimensional change)
- Spec: MIL-DTL-13924

**Best for:** Steel parts where dimensional change from plating is unacceptable. Tooling, fixtures, gauges, military applications. Provides mild corrosion resistance — often supplemented with oil or wax.

## SECTION: Heat Treatment

**H2:** Heat Treatment

### Hardening & Tempering

**What it does:** Heats steel above critical temperature, quenches rapidly (oil or water), then tempers at lower temperature to reduce brittleness while retaining hardness.

**Result:** Hardness from HRC 40 to HRC 65 depending on steel grade and temper temperature.

**Best for:** Parts that need wear resistance and strength — gears, shafts, tooling, dies, cams.

---

### Case Hardening (Carburizing)

**What it does:** Diffuses carbon into the surface of low-carbon steel at high temperature. Creates a hard outer shell (HRC 58–62) with a tough, ductile core.

**Case depth:** 0.010"–0.060" typical

**Best for:** Parts that need a wear-resistant surface but must absorb impact without fracturing. Gears, shafts, pins, cams, rollers.

---

### Stress Relief

**What it does:** Heats the part below transformation temperature (typically 1000–1200°F) and cools slowly. Removes residual stresses from machining without changing hardness or microstructure.

**Best for:** Precision parts with tight tolerances. Machining introduces internal stress that can cause warping over time — especially on thin-walled or asymmetric parts. Stress relief stabilizes the part dimensionally.

---

### Annealing

**What it does:** Heats the part above transformation temperature and cools very slowly. Softens the material, relieves all internal stress, and improves machinability.

**Best for:** Pre-machining treatment for parts that will undergo heavy material removal. Also used between machining operations on parts with demanding tolerances.

---

## SECTION: Marking

**H2:** Marking & Identification

### Laser Marking

**What it does:** Focused laser beam creates permanent marks on the surface — engraved, etched, annealed, or color-changed depending on material and settings.

**Marks:** Part numbers, serial numbers, logos, date codes, barcodes, QR codes, 2D data matrix

**Materials:** All metals and most plastics

**Best for:** Permanent traceability marking. Required for aerospace (per AS9102), medical (per UDI requirements), and automotive (per IATF 16949 traceability).

---

### Engraving

**What it does:** Mechanical or laser removal of material to create deep, tactile marks.

**Best for:** Harsh environments where surface marks could wear away. Die stamps, mold identification, heavy equipment.

---

### Silk Screening

**What it does:** Ink transferred through a fine mesh screen onto flat surfaces. Multi-color graphics, labels, and text.

**Best for:** Control panel markings, front panel labels, equipment identification plates, multi-color graphics on enclosures.

## SECTION: FAQ

**H2:** Common Questions About Surface Finishing

**Q: Can you apply multiple finishes on the same part?**
Yes. Common combinations: bead blast + anodize (uniform cosmetic anodize), machining + heat treatment + grinding (hardened precision parts), nickel plate + chrome plate (multi-layer corrosion/wear protection). We sequence operations in the right order.

**Q: Do finishes affect part dimensions?**
Yes — most finishes add thickness. Anodize Type II adds 0.0005" per side. Hard coat adds up to 0.0015" per side. Plating varies by thickness spec. We account for this in our DFM review and specify pre-finish dimensions on critical features.

**Q: Which finish is the cheapest?**
Bead blasting and black oxide are the lowest-cost options. Powder coating is the best value for color + protection. Zinc plating is the standard low-cost corrosion solution for steel.

**Q: Which finish is best for outdoor use?**
Powder coating (any metal) or anodize Type II/III (aluminum). Both provide years of outdoor durability with UV resistance.

**Q: Do you provide finish certifications?**
Yes. Anodize per MIL-A-8625, chem film per MIL-DTL-5541, plating per ASTM B633 (zinc) or applicable spec. Certification documents accompany every shipment when specified.
