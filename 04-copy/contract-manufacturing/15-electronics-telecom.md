## SECTION: Challenges

**Label:** What Electronics Manufacturing Demands

**H2:** Electronics Manufacturing Challenges

Electronics machining lives at the intersection of thermal physics, electromagnetic compatibility, and mechanical precision. The parts are smaller, the tolerances are tighter, and the materials are chosen for their physical properties — not just their strength.

**Thermal management** — Every watt of power dissipated by a processor, MOSFET, or power supply becomes heat. That heat must be conducted away from the component, spread across a heat sink, and convected into the air. The heat sink's fin geometry, surface area, flatness, and contact surface finish directly determine whether the system runs at 60°C or 90°C. A 30°C difference is the difference between a 10-year product life and a 2-year one.

**EMI/RFI shielding** — Electronic enclosures are not just boxes. They are Faraday cages. The material, wall thickness, seam design, and surface finish determine how much electromagnetic interference leaks in or out. A machined aluminum enclosure with proper gasket grooves and conductive finish blocks interference that a sheet metal box with gaps cannot.

**Tight tolerances on interfaces** — PCB mounting holes that are off by 0.005" mean the board does not seat. Connector cutouts that are oversized by 0.010" mean the connector wobbles. Mating surfaces that are not flat to 0.001" mean thermal paste gaps and hot spots. Every feature on an electronics enclosure interfaces with something — and every interface has a tolerance.

**Miniaturization** — Components shrink. Boards get denser. Enclosures get smaller. The machining features get smaller too — smaller pockets, thinner walls, finer threads, closer hole spacing. What was comfortable at 0.060" wall thickness becomes challenging at 0.030".

**Cosmetic requirements** — Consumer-facing electronics products need clean, consistent surfaces. Anodized aluminum with no tool marks, no scratches, and uniform color across 500 parts. The enclosure is the first thing the user sees — it sets the perception of quality for everything inside.

## SECTION: Heat Sinks

**H2:** Heat Sink Machining — A Core Capability

Heat sinks are one of GPW's highest-volume electronics parts. They combine tight tolerances, complex geometry, and material properties that demand CNC expertise.

**Types we machine:**

| Heat Sink Type | Description | Best For |
|---------------|-------------|----------|
| Straight-fin | Parallel fins machined from billet. Simplest, lowest cost. | Forced-air cooling with unidirectional airflow |
| Pin-fin | Array of cylindrical or square pins. Omnidirectional airflow. | Natural convection or multi-directional forced air |
| Cross-cut fin | Straight fins with perpendicular cuts creating a grid pattern. | Turbulent airflow, improved mixing |
| Custom profile | Application-specific geometry — stepped, tapered, curved fins | Constrained spaces, optimized thermal paths |

**Critical dimensions on heat sinks:**
- **Base flatness:** 0.001"–0.002" across the contact surface. This is where the heat sink meets the component via thermal interface material. Any deviation creates an air gap that blocks heat transfer.
- **Fin thickness:** As thin as 0.020" (0.5mm) depending on material and height. Thinner fins = more surface area = better cooling, but harder to machine without deflection.
- **Fin height-to-gap ratio:** Tall, closely spaced fins are harder to machine — the end mill deflects. Our DFM review optimizes this ratio for your thermal requirement vs machining feasibility.
- **Surface finish on contact face:** Ra 0.8 µm or better for thermal paste interface. Smoother = thinner thermal interface = lower thermal resistance.

**Material selection for heat sinks:**

| Material | Thermal Conductivity | When to Use |
|----------|---------------------|-------------|
| Aluminum 6063 | 201 W/m·K | Best surface finish for anodizing. Standard for most heat sinks. |
| Aluminum 6061 | 167 W/m·K | When structural strength matters more than thermal performance. |
| Copper 101 (OFHC) | 391 W/m·K | Maximum thermal performance. 2× the conductivity of aluminum. 3× the weight and cost. |
| Copper 110 | 388 W/m·K | Nearly equal to 101 at lower cost. Use when oxygen-free purity is not required. |

"Anodized aluminum radiates heat better than bare aluminum — emissivity of 0.85 vs 0.05. If your heat sink relies on radiation (natural convection), anodize it. If it is under forced air, anodizing is optional."

## SECTION: EMI Enclosures

**H2:** EMI/RFI Shielding Enclosures

An electronics enclosure is only as good as its shielding effectiveness. Gaps, seams, and improper surface finishes turn a Faraday cage into a sieve.

**What makes a machined enclosure better than sheet metal for EMI:**
- **Continuous walls** — No seams, no welds, no gaps for EMI to leak through. A machined enclosure from a single billet has zero joints on 5 of 6 faces.
- **Gasket grooves** — Precision-machined grooves for conductive EMI gaskets. Consistent depth and width across the entire perimeter = consistent compression = consistent shielding.
- **Flatness** — Machined mating surfaces flat to 0.001"–0.002" ensure gasket contact across the full seam. No high spots, no low spots, no leakage paths.

**Surface finishes for EMI shielding:**

| Finish | Shielding Performance | Notes |
|--------|----------------------|-------|
| Chem film (Alodine) | Good | Conductive, low cost, MIL-DTL-5541. Maintains grounding. |
| Electroless nickel | Excellent | Uniform coverage, good conductivity, corrosion resistant |
| Zinc-nickel plating | Excellent | High corrosion resistance + good conductivity |
| Bare machined aluminum | Poor | Aluminum oxide is non-conductive. Must be coated for EMI. |

"If your enclosure needs EMI shielding, do NOT anodize the mating surfaces — anodize is non-conductive. Use chem film or nickel plate on shielding surfaces, and anodize only the external cosmetic surfaces."
