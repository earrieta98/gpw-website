## SECTION: Why Material Matters

**Label:** The Right Metal for the Job

**H2:** Why Material Selection Matters

The metal you choose determines how your part performs, how long it lasts, how much it costs to machine, and how much it costs to buy. Over-specifying wastes money. Under-specifying causes failures.

Two parts that look identical on a drawing can cost 3× different depending on the material. Aluminum 6061 machines in half the time of stainless 316 and costs a third as much per pound. But if your part lives in a saltwater environment, 6061 will corrode in months while 316 lasts decades.

The goal is matching the material to the application — not picking the most expensive option and not picking the cheapest.

## SECTION: Aluminum

**H2:** Aluminum Alloys

Aluminum is the most commonly machined metal — roughly 40% of all CNC jobs. Lightweight, corrosion-resistant, excellent machinability, and available in dozens of alloys for different applications.

### Aluminum 6061-T6

The default aluminum for CNC machining. If you are not sure which aluminum to specify, start here.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 45,000 PSI (310 MPa) |
| Yield Strength | 40,000 PSI (276 MPa) |
| Hardness | 95 HB |
| Density | 0.098 lb/in³ (2.70 g/cm³) |
| Machinability | Excellent — fast feeds, clean chips, low tool wear |
| Corrosion Resistance | Good — excellent with anodize |
| Weldability | Good |

**Best for:** General-purpose structural parts. Brackets, housings, mounting plates, frames, fixtures, heat sinks, enclosures. The combination of strength, machinability, availability, and cost makes it the right choice for most applications.

**Industries:** All — aerospace, automotive, industrial, electronics, medical, consumer

**Finishing:** Anodize Type II (color + corrosion), anodize Type III (wear), powder coat, chem film, bead blast

---

### Aluminum 7075-T6

The high-strength aluminum. Use when 6061 is not strong enough and steel is too heavy.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 83,000 PSI (572 MPa) |
| Yield Strength | 73,000 PSI (503 MPa) |
| Hardness | 150 HB |
| Density | 0.101 lb/in³ (2.81 g/cm³) |
| Machinability | Good — harder on tooling than 6061, still machines well |
| Corrosion Resistance | Fair — less than 6061, needs anodize or chem film |
| Weldability | Poor — avoid welding 7075 |

**Best for:** High-stress structural components where weight matters. Aerospace brackets, fittings, and ribs. Tooling plates. Sporting goods. Anything that needs to be strong and light.

**Cost note:** 7075 costs 2–3× more than 6061 per pound. Only specify when the strength justifies it.

**Industries:** Aerospace, defense, sporting goods, high-performance automotive

---

### Aluminum 2024

Fatigue-resistant aluminum. Developed for aircraft structures that see repeated loading cycles.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 68,000 PSI (469 MPa) |
| Fatigue Strength | Superior to 6061 and 7075 |
| Corrosion Resistance | Poor — requires cladding or coating |
| Machinability | Good |

**Best for:** Aircraft fuselage skins, wing ribs, fatigue-critical structural members. Not for general use — 6061 or 7075 cover most applications better.

**Industries:** Aerospace

---

### Aluminum 5052

The formability and corrosion champion. Best aluminum for sheet metal applications and marine environments.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 33,000 PSI (228 MPa) |
| Corrosion Resistance | Excellent — best of common aluminum alloys |
| Formability | Excellent — bends without cracking |
| Machinability | Good |

**Best for:** Sheet metal enclosures, marine hardware, fuel tanks, chemical containers. When corrosion resistance and formability matter more than strength.

**Industries:** Marine, industrial, sheet metal fabrication

---

### Aluminum 6063

The surface finish aluminum. Machines to a smoother finish than 6061 and anodizes with a more uniform appearance.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 35,000 PSI (241 MPa) |
| Corrosion Resistance | Excellent |
| Surface Finish | Superior anodizing quality |
| Machinability | Good |

**Best for:** Architectural parts, cosmetic enclosures, complex heat sink fins (often starts as an extrusion, then CNC-machined to final shape). When anodized appearance matters.

**Industries:** Electronics (heat sinks), consumer products, architectural

## SECTION: Steel

**H2:** Steel Alloys

Steel is the workhorse of industrial machining — strong, tough, heat-treatable, and available in hundreds of grades. Heavier than aluminum but significantly stronger.

### Steel 1018 (Mild / Low Carbon)

The default low-cost steel. Easy to machine, easy to weld, readily available.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 63,000 PSI (440 MPa) |
| Hardness | 126 HB (as-rolled) |
| Machinability | Excellent — smooth cutting, good surface finish |
| Weldability | Excellent |
| Heat Treatment | Case hardenable (not through-hardenable) |

**Best for:** Non-critical structural parts, pins, shafts, spacers, fixtures, jigs, custom fasteners. The go-to steel when strength requirements are moderate and cost matters.

**Industries:** Industrial, automotive, general manufacturing

---

### Steel 4140 (Alloy)

The medium-carbon alloy steel for parts that need real strength. Heat-treatable to a wide range of hardnesses.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 95,000 PSI annealed / 150,000+ PSI heat-treated |
| Hardness | 197 HB annealed / 50+ HRC heat-treated |
| Machinability | Good in annealed condition |
| Weldability | Fair — preheat required |
| Heat Treatment | Through-hardenable — oil or water quench |

**Best for:** Gears, shafts, axles, connecting rods, tooling, heavy-duty structural. Parts that see high loads and need to be hard and tough.

**Industries:** Automotive, oil & gas, industrial equipment, heavy machinery

---

### Steel 4340 (Alloy)

The highest strength common steel. When 4140 is not enough.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 108,000 PSI annealed / 180,000+ PSI heat-treated |
| Fatigue Strength | Superior to 4140 |
| Toughness | Excellent — retains ductility at high hardness |
| Machinability | Fair — slower than 4140, harder on tooling |

**Best for:** Landing gear, crankshafts, high-stress aerospace fasteners, drill collars, tool joints. The steel you choose when failure is not an option.

**Industries:** Aerospace, defense, oil & gas, heavy industrial

**Cost note:** More expensive than 4140 and slower to machine. Use when fatigue or impact loading requires it.

---

### Steel A36 (Structural)

Low-cost structural steel. The cheapest common steel.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 58,000–80,000 PSI |
| Machinability | Good |
| Weldability | Excellent |

**Best for:** Structural brackets, weldments, base plates, non-precision parts where material properties are not critical. Often used for welded frames and structures.

**Industries:** Construction, industrial equipment, general fabrication

## SECTION: Stainless Steel

**H2:** Stainless Steel

Stainless steel resists corrosion through its chromium content (minimum 10.5%). Different grades balance machinability, strength, and corrosion resistance differently.

### Stainless 303

The machinist's stainless. Added sulfur makes it the fastest-cutting stainless grade.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 85,000 PSI |
| Machinability | Best of any stainless — designed for high-speed turning |
| Corrosion Resistance | Good — but less than 304 or 316 |
| Weldability | Poor — not recommended for welding |

**Best for:** High-volume turned parts where machinability drives cost. Fittings, fasteners, shafts, valve stems, nuts.

**When to use 303 over 304:** When you do not need to weld it and the corrosion environment is not severe. 303 machines 2–3× faster than 304.

---

### Stainless 304

The most widely used stainless. The default when someone says "stainless steel."

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 75,000 PSI |
| Corrosion Resistance | Excellent — handles most environments |
| Weldability | Excellent |
| Machinability | Fair — work-hardens, stringy chips, harder on tooling |

**Best for:** Food and beverage equipment, medical devices, chemical handling, kitchen equipment, outdoor hardware. Any application requiring corrosion resistance and weldability.

**Industries:** Medical, food, pharmaceutical, industrial, consumer

---

### Stainless 316

The corrosion champion. When 304 is not enough.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 75,000 PSI |
| Corrosion Resistance | Superior — resists chlorides, saltwater, acids |
| Machinability | Fair — similar to 304, slightly harder |
| Cost | 20–30% more than 304 |

**Best for:** Marine hardware, chemical processing, pharmaceutical equipment, surgical instruments, any application with chloride or acid exposure.

**When to use 316 over 304:** Marine/saltwater environments, chemical exposure to chlorides or acids, pharmaceutical/medical where pitting corrosion is unacceptable.

**Industries:** Medical, marine, pharmaceutical, chemical processing

---

### Stainless 17-4PH

The strongest common stainless. Precipitation-hardened — heat-treat to high hardness while retaining corrosion resistance.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | Up to 190,000 PSI (heat-treated) |
| Hardness | Up to 44 HRC |
| Corrosion Resistance | Good — better than 410, less than 316 |
| Machinability | Fair — machines in solution-annealed condition, then heat-treated |

**Best for:** Aerospace fasteners, valve components, gears, shafts, pump components. When you need both strength and corrosion resistance in the same part.

**Industries:** Aerospace, oil & gas, medical, defense

---

### Stainless 440C

The hardest common stainless. Can be hardened to 58–60 HRC — comparable to tool steel.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Hardness | 58–60 HRC (heat-treated) |
| Corrosion Resistance | Moderate — less than 304/316, but corrosion-resistant for a hard material |
| Machinability | Machine in annealed state, then harden |

**Best for:** Bearings, valve seats, bushings, cutting edges, wear surfaces that also need corrosion resistance. Wire EDM after hardening for tight-tolerance features.

**Industries:** Medical (surgical instruments), industrial (bearings, valves)

## SECTION: Titanium

**H2:** Titanium

Titanium offers the highest strength-to-weight ratio of any structural metal. It is also biocompatible, corrosion-immune, and expensive.

### Titanium Grade 2 (Commercially Pure)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 50,000 PSI |
| Density | 0.163 lb/in³ (4.51 g/cm³) — 60% the weight of steel |
| Corrosion Resistance | Excellent — virtually immune in most environments |
| Biocompatibility | Yes — per ISO 10993 |
| Machinability | Moderate — requires sharp tooling, low speeds, rigid setup |

**Best for:** Chemical processing equipment, marine hardware, non-structural medical implants. When corrosion immunity matters more than strength.

---

### Titanium Grade 5 (Ti-6Al-4V)

The most used titanium alloy. 60% of all titanium production goes to this grade.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tensile Strength | 130,000 PSI (900 MPa) |
| Density | 0.160 lb/in³ (4.43 g/cm³) |
| Corrosion Resistance | Excellent |
| Biocompatibility | Yes — the standard for orthopedic implants |
| Machinability | Difficult — low thermal conductivity, work-hardening, tool wear |

**Best for:** Aerospace structural components, medical implants (hip, knee, spinal), fasteners, engine parts. When you need the strength of steel at 60% of the weight.

**Cost note:** Titanium Grade 5 costs 10–20× more per pound than aluminum 6061 and machines 3–5× slower. Specify only when the application demands it.

**Industries:** Aerospace, medical, defense, high-performance sports

## SECTION: Copper, Brass, Bronze

**H2:** Copper, Brass & Bronze

### Brass 360 (Free-Machining)

The fastest-machining common metal. Period.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Machinability | 100 (the baseline — all other metals are rated against brass 360) |
| Electrical Conductivity | 26% IACS |
| Corrosion Resistance | Good |

**Best for:** Fittings, valves, connectors, electrical terminals, plumbing components, decorative hardware. When you need thousands of turned parts fast and cheap.

**Industries:** Plumbing, electrical, industrial, automotive

---

### Copper 101 (Oxygen-Free, OFHC)

The electrical and thermal conductivity champion.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Electrical Conductivity | 101% IACS (highest of any common metal) |
| Thermal Conductivity | 226 BTU/hr·ft·°F |
| Machinability | Fair — soft and gummy, requires sharp tooling |

**Best for:** Bus bars, heat sinks, waveguides, electrical contacts. Any application where maximizing electrical or thermal conductivity is the primary requirement.

**Industries:** Electronics, electrical, telecommunications

---

### Copper 110 (ETP — Electrolytic Tough Pitch)

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Electrical Conductivity | 100% IACS |
| Availability | More common and less expensive than 101 |

**Best for:** General electrical components, connectors, terminals. Use when 101's oxygen-free property is not required.

---

### Bronze 932 (SAE 660)

The bearing material.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Bearing Properties | Self-lubricating, excellent wear resistance |
| Compressive Strength | 30,000 PSI |
| Machinability | Good |

**Best for:** Bushings, bearings, thrust washers, wear plates, slide pads. Runs against hardened steel shafts without galling.

**Industries:** Industrial machinery, automotive, marine, heavy equipment

## SECTION: Tool Steel

**H2:** Tool Steel

Tool steels are designed for making tools — dies, molds, punches, and gauges. They are hard, wear-resistant, and dimensionally stable. Most tool steels are machined in the annealed (soft) state, then hardened, then finished by grinding or wire EDM.

### D2

| Hardness | 58–62 HRC | High wear resistance, air-hardening |
|----------|-----------|------|
**Best for:** Blanking dies, forming dies, punches, slitter knives, wear-resistant tooling. The most common die steel.

### A2

| Hardness | 57–62 HRC | Good toughness + wear balance, air-hardening |
|----------|-----------|------|
**Best for:** Punches, dies, tooling where toughness matters more than extreme wear resistance. Less brittle than D2.

### H13

| Hardness | 44–52 HRC | Hot-work steel, retains hardness at elevated temperatures |
|----------|-----------|------|
**Best for:** Die casting dies, extrusion tooling, hot forging dies, injection mold cores. Any tooling that operates at high temperature.

### S7

| Hardness | 54–58 HRC | Shock-resistant, highest impact toughness |
|----------|-----------|------|
**Best for:** Chisels, punches, shear blades, impact tooling. When the tool takes hits.

### O1

| Hardness | 57–62 HRC | Oil-hardening, fine grain structure, excellent dimensional stability |
|----------|-----------|------|
**Best for:** Gauges, jigs, fixtures, precision tooling, cutting tools, taps, reamers. When dimensional precision after hardening is critical.

## SECTION: Selection Guide

**H2:** Metal Selection Guide

| Your Need | Recommended Metal | Why |
|-----------|------------------|-----|
| General-purpose lightweight | Aluminum 6061-T6 | Best all-around: strong, machinable, affordable |
| Maximum strength (lightweight) | Aluminum 7075-T6 | Near-steel strength at 1/3 the weight |
| General-purpose steel | Steel 1018 | Cheap, easy to machine, easy to weld |
| High-strength steel | Steel 4140 or 4340 | Heat-treatable, tough |
| Corrosion resistant (standard) | Stainless 304 | Handles most environments |
| Corrosion resistant (harsh) | Stainless 316 | Chlorides, acids, marine |
| High strength + corrosion | Stainless 17-4PH | When you need both |
| Maximum machinability (stainless) | Stainless 303 | 2–3× faster than 304 |
| Lightweight + biocompatible | Titanium Grade 5 | Aerospace, medical implants |
| Electrical conductivity | Copper 101 or 110 | Highest conductivity available |
| Fast high-volume turning | Brass 360 | Fastest machining metal |
| Bearings and wear surfaces | Bronze 932 | Self-lubricating |
| Tooling and dies | D2, A2, or H13 | Depends on application temp and impact |

## SECTION: FAQ

**H2:** Common Questions About CNC Machining Metals

**Q: What is the most commonly requested metal?**
Aluminum 6061-T6 — approximately 40% of all CNC machining projects. It is the default choice when the application does not specifically demand something else.

**Q: Which metal is cheapest to machine?**
Brass 360 and aluminum 6061 are the fastest and cheapest to machine. Steel 1018 is the cheapest steel option. Stainless 303 is the cheapest stainless option per cycle time.

**Q: Can you machine hardened steel?**
Yes — via wire EDM. Conventional machining of hardened steel (above 45 HRC) destroys cutting tools. The typical approach: machine in annealed state → heat treat → finish critical features by EDM or grinding.
