## SECTION: DFM Review

**H2:** DFM Review — Design for Manufacturability

**What it is:** A technical review of your CAD file or drawing before production. We analyze your design through the lens of the machining process — not just "can we make this?" but "what is the most efficient way to make this?"

**What we check:**

- **Tolerances** — Are all tolerances functionally necessary? A ±0.001" tolerance costs 3–5× more than ±0.005". If a surface mates with nothing, ±0.005" is sufficient. We flag every tolerance that appears tighter than the application requires.

- **Wall thickness** — Thin walls deflect under cutting forces, require slower feeds, and increase scrap risk. We recommend minimum wall thickness based on your material and part geometry.

- **Internal radii** — Sharp internal corners require smaller tools, slower speeds, and sometimes EDM. Increasing a corner radius from 0.5mm to 1.5mm can reduce cycle time by 20% on some parts. We flag corners where a larger radius does not affect function.

- **Undercuts and deep pockets** — Features that require special tooling, extended-reach cutters, or additional setups. We identify whether these features can be redesigned to machine from a standard setup.

- **Number of setups** — Every time the part is re-fixtured, you pay for setup time and lose positional accuracy between features. We look for opportunities to reduce setups by reorienting features or suggesting live tooling on the lathe.

- **Material selection** — Is the specified material the best choice for the application? Sometimes a different alloy machines faster, costs less, or performs better. We recommend alternatives with data — not opinions.

**What you get:** An annotated review with specific recommendations. Not a generic "your part looks good" — actionable feedback with estimated cost impact per suggestion.

## SECTION: Value Engineering

**H2:** Value Engineering — Reduce Cost on Parts You Already Make

DFM reviews apply to new designs. Value Engineering applies to parts already in production — parts you have been ordering from your current supplier for months or years.

**What it is:** We analyze your existing part — the drawing, the current process, the material, the tolerances — and find opportunities to reduce per-piece cost without changing how the part functions.

**Common value engineering findings:**

**Tolerance relaxation**
"This diameter is specified at ±0.0005" but it's a clearance bore with 0.010" of play in the assembly. Relaxing to ±0.002" eliminates a grinding operation and reduces cost 25%."

**Material substitution**
"This bracket is 7075 aluminum but it carries no structural load. Switching to 6061 cuts material cost 40% and machines 15% faster."

**Part consolidation**
"These two parts bolt together and are always used as a pair. Machining them as one piece eliminates 4 fasteners, 2 assembly steps, and 1 drawing."

**Process change**
"This feature is currently done on a 5-axis mill because of a 15° angle on one face. Adding a 15° fixture allows it to run on a 3-axis machine at half the cycle time."

**When to use Value Engineering:** You already have parts in production and want to reduce cost. You are switching suppliers and want to re-evaluate the current approach. You are scaling volume and need to reduce per-unit cost to hit your target margin.

## SECTION: Material Selection

**H2:** Material Selection Guidance

Choosing the wrong material is expensive. Over-specifying wastes money on material and machining time. Under-specifying leads to failures in the field.

GPW's engineering team helps you select the right material based on:

- **Mechanical requirements** — What loads does the part see? Tension, compression, shear, fatigue, impact?
- **Environment** — Temperature range, chemical exposure, humidity, UV, saltwater?
- **Machinability** — Some materials machine 5× faster than others. If your performance requirements can be met by a more machinable alloy, you save on cycle time.
- **Weight** — Aluminum is 1/3 the weight of steel. Titanium is half. PEEK replaces metal in some applications. We quantify the tradeoff.
- **Cost** — Material cost per pound, plus the cost of machining it. Titanium is expensive to buy AND expensive to machine. Sometimes stainless 17-4PH gives you the same performance at half the total cost.
- **Availability** — Exotic alloys have long lead times. If a standard alloy meets your requirements, you ship weeks faster.

**Common decisions we help with:**

| Decision | Factors |
|----------|---------|
| 6061 vs 7075 aluminum | Strength vs cost vs machinability |
| 304 vs 316 stainless | Corrosion environment (316 for chloride/marine) |
| 303 vs 304 stainless | Machinability vs weldability vs corrosion |
| Steel 4140 vs 4340 | Strength vs toughness vs fatigue life |
| Titanium vs 17-4PH stainless | Weight vs cost vs machinability |
| Delrin vs Nylon | Dimensional stability vs impact strength |
| PEEK vs Ultem | Temperature vs cost vs sterilization |
| Metal vs engineering plastic | Weight, corrosion, electrical insulation, cost |

## SECTION: Process Recommendation

**H2:** Process Recommendation

Not every buyer knows whether their part should be milled, turned, EDM-cut, or fabricated from sheet metal. That is not their job — it is ours.

GPW's engineering team evaluates your part geometry and recommends the right process:

| Your Part | Recommended Process | Why |
|-----------|-------------------|-----|
| Complex 3D geometry, pockets, contours | CNC Milling | Multi-axis capability, wide range of features |
| Round/cylindrical, shafts, pins | CNC Turning | Faster and cheaper than milling round parts |
| Hardened steel, sharp internal corners | Wire EDM | Cuts any hardness, corners sharper than tool radius |
| Flat stock, bends, welded assemblies | Sheet Metal | Cut, bend, weld — lower cost than machining from block |
| Combination of features | Multi-process | We coordinate: turn → mill → finish → ship |

Many parts combine two or more processes. A shaft might be turned on the lathe, then have flats milled, then be heat-treated, then ground to final diameter. We coordinate the full sequence and deliver the finished part.

## SECTION: What Makes This Different

**H2:** This Is Not Automated DFM

Platforms like Xometry and Protolabs offer automated DFM feedback. Their software flags thin walls, small holes, and undercuts — useful, but generic. It does not know your application. It cannot suggest a different alloy. It cannot tell you that combining two operations into one setup saves 30%.

GPW's engineering review is performed by a person who machines parts for a living. The feedback is specific to your part, your material, your tolerances, and your application. It comes with cost estimates — not just "this feature is flagged."

**Q: Is the DFM review really free?**
Yes. On every RFQ, without exception. We do it because it saves you money and makes the project go smoother — which means fewer quality issues, fewer back-and-forth cycles, and a better relationship for both sides.

**Q: What file formats do you accept?**
STEP (.stp), IGES (.igs), DXF, DWG, PDF drawings. Solidworks, Fusion 360, and other native formats accepted. If all you have is a sketch on paper, take a photo and send it — we have worked with less.

**Q: What if I do not have a CAD file?**
Send a drawing, a sketch with dimensions, or the physical part. We can reverse-engineer existing parts — measure them, create CAD, and manufacture from there.

Yes. We sign your NDA or provide ours before receiving any files. Standard practice on every project.

**Q: How detailed is the DFM feedback?**
Specific and actionable. Not "this wall is thin" — instead: "This wall is 0.8mm which will deflect during machining in 7075 aluminum. Increasing to 1.5mm eliminates the need for custom fixturing and reduces scrap risk. Estimated cost impact: -15% per part."

**Q: Can you help with parts I already have in production?**
Yes — that is Value Engineering. Send us your current drawing and production part. We analyze the design and process to identify cost reduction opportunities.
