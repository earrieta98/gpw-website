## SECTION: Challenges

**Label:** What Medical Manufacturing Demands

**H2:** Medical Device Manufacturing Challenges

Medical machining is not just tight tolerances — it is tight tolerances with a regulatory framework wrapped around every step. The part itself is half the deliverable. The documentation that proves it was made correctly is the other half.

**Biocompatibility** — Materials that contact the human body must be safe. Titanium Grade 5 osseointegrates with bone. Stainless 316L resists corrosion inside the body. PEEK is radiolucent — invisible to X-ray so surgeons can see the anatomy around the implant. Using the wrong material is not a warranty claim. It is a patient safety event.

**Surface finish** — An implant surface with machining scratches harbors bacteria. A fluid pathway with burrs creates turbulence and particle generation. Medical parts are finished to Ra 0.4 µm or better — often to Ra 0.2 µm via electropolishing. The finish is a functional requirement, not cosmetic.

**Traceability** — Every part must trace from finished device back through inspection data, machine, operator, date, process parameters, and raw material lot. This is the Device History Record (DHR) — and it must be complete, accurate, and retrievable for the life of the device.

**Regulatory burden** — FDA 21 CFR Part 820 requires design controls, process validation, and documented CAPA systems. ISO 13485 requires a quality management system purpose-built for medical devices. These are not suggestions — they are conditions for selling medical devices in the U.S. and globally.

**Small lots, high documentation** — A clinical trial might need 25 parts. A commercial launch might need 200 per month. Both require the same documentation rigor. The cost of documentation per part is high at low volumes — which is why machining costs matter even more in medical.

## SECTION: Materials

**H2:** Materials for Medical Devices

Medical material selection is not just about mechanical properties — it is about biocompatibility, sterilization compatibility, and regulatory classification.

| Material | Medical Applications | Key Properties | Biocompatible |
|----------|---------------------|----------------|---------------|
| Stainless 316L | Surgical instruments, reusable devices, orthopedic tools | Corrosion resistant, sterilizable (autoclave, EtO, gamma) | Yes |
| Stainless 17-4PH | High-strength instrument components, forceps, clamps | Heat-treatable to 190,000 PSI + corrosion resistance | Yes |
| Stainless 440C | Cutting edges, blade inserts, wear surfaces | Hardened to 60 HRC, holds a sharp edge | Limited — surface contact only |
| Titanium Grade 2 | Non-structural implants, bone plates, hardware | Biocompatible, corrosion-immune, lower cost than Gr5 | Yes — ISO 10993 |
| Titanium Grade 5 | Load-bearing implants, spinal cages, hip/knee components | 130,000 PSI, osseointegrates with bone, radiopaque | Yes — ISO 10993 |
| PEEK | Spinal fusion cages, bearing surfaces, radiolucent implants | Invisible to X-ray, strong, biocompatible, chemical resistant | Yes — implant grades |
| Delrin / Acetal | Drug delivery components, instrument handles, guides | FDA-compliant, low friction, autoclavable | FDA food-contact |
| Ultem / PEI | Sterilizable housings, reusable connectors, trays | Withstands 1,000+ autoclave cycles without degradation | Yes — autoclave stable |

**Medical-grade materials:**
- Titanium: sourced per ASTM F136 (Grade 5 ELI for implants) or ASTM F67 (Grade 2 CP)
- PEEK: Invibio PEEK-OPTIMA or Solvay KetaSpire for implant-grade applications
- Stainless: sourced per ASTM F138 (316L surgical) or ASTM A276 (instrument grades)

Material certifications with biocompatibility documentation per ISO 10993 accompany every shipment when specified.

## SECTION: Quality

**H2:** Quality & Compliance

Medical device quality is not a department — it is the entire manufacturing process. Every step is documented, every decision is traceable, and every deviation is investigated.

**ISO 13485**
Medical device quality management system. GPW is on the ISO 13485 pathway — our processes follow 13485-aligned procedures. We provide the documentation that 13485 requires on every medical project.

**FDA 21 CFR Part 820**
Quality System Regulation for medical devices sold in the United States. Requires design controls, process validation, documented procedures, and corrective/preventive action (CAPA) systems.

**Device History Record (DHR)**
Complete manufacturing documentation for every lot:

| DHR Element | What GPW Provides |

## SECTION: Why Nearshore

**H2:** Why Nearshore Medical Device Manufacturing?

**Faster iteration for device development** — Medical devices go through multiple design iterations before design freeze. Each iteration needs prototype parts in production materials — not 3D-printed approximations — for functional testing, bench testing, and cadaver studies. GPW delivers prototype parts in 1–2 weeks, not 4–6.

**Lower cost prototypes and clinical trial quantities** — Pre-market R&D budgets are tight. A clinical trial might need 50 titanium implants. At 60% labor savings versus domestic machining, GPW makes your R&D budget go further.

**Same timezone for design collaboration** — When your engineer has a question about a radius on a spinal cage at 2 PM, our engineer answers at 2 PM — not at 2 AM tomorrow. Real-time collaboration during the design phase eliminates revision cycles.

**USMCA tariff advantage** — Medical device components manufactured in Mexico under USMCA enter the U.S. with preferential tariff treatment. No Section 301 exposure like China-sourced components.

**Mexico's medical device ecosystem** — Mexico's medical device manufacturing sector generates $15B+ annually. Baja California alone hosts 70+ medical device manufacturers. Regulatory agencies (COFEPRIS) align with FDA frameworks. U.S. FDA regularly audits Mexican facilities — this is established territory, not frontier.

**Proximity for supplier audits** — Monterrey is a 2-hour flight from Minneapolis (Medtronic), Boston (Stryker, DePuy), San Jose (Intuitive Surgical), Memphis (Smith & Nephew), and Warsaw, IN (Zimmer Biomet via Chicago connection). Your quality team can audit in person without crossing an ocean.
