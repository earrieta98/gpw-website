# Product Marketing Context — GPW Electromechanical Assembly (EMS)

*Last updated: March 2026*

## Product Overview
**One-liner:** GPW is a nearshore electromechanical assembly partner in Monterrey, Mexico, engineered to deliver precision builds for U.S. OEMs — from AI servers to industrial box builds and multi-industry integration projects.
**What it does:** GPW provides full-service electromechanical assembly as a contract service for original equipment manufacturers. Core capabilities include AI server rack assembly, cable harness and wire assembly, box build integration, electromechanical sub-assembly, component integration onto boards and chassis, and end-of-line testing & QA. GPW does not fabricate PCBAs in-house but can subcontract PCBA services when a project requires it. GPW operates from Monterrey — just 2 hours from the U.S. border by road — offering same-timezone collaboration, USMCA-compliant supply chain, and scalable production capacity for Tier 1 and Tier 2 OEMs seeking to nearshore manufacturing from Asia or supplement domestic U.S. capacity.
**Product category:** Electronic Manufacturing Services (EMS) / Contract Electromechanical Assembly / Nearshore Manufacturing
**Product type:** B2B industrial service (contract manufacturing)
**Business model:** Project-based and recurring contract assembly. Revenue from per-unit assembly fees, NRE (non-recurring engineering) charges, and ongoing production contracts. Pricing is quote-based per RFQ (Request for Quote).

## Target Audience
**Target companies:** U.S.-based OEMs across multiple verticals: AI/Data Center infrastructure, Industrial (equipment, controls, light machinery), Telecommunications (hardware, cabinets, modules), Medical (devices/equipment, controlled assembly), Automotive (sub-assemblies, fixtures, integration — not heavy Tier 1), Energy (equipment, cabinets, integration), Aerospace (sub-assemblies with traceability focus), and Appliances/White Goods. Company size ranges from mid-market ($50M–$500M revenue) to enterprise. Primarily companies currently manufacturing in Asia (China, Taiwan, Vietnam) looking to nearshore, or domestic U.S. manufacturers looking to reduce costs while keeping supply chain close.

**Primary use case:** OEM needs a reliable contract assembly partner in Mexico to build electromechanical products — especially AI server racks and complex assemblies — with quality, speed, and cost advantages vs. domestic U.S. or offshore Asia production.

## Problems & Pain Points
**Core problem:** U.S. OEMs face a triple squeeze — rising costs of domestic assembly, long lead times and geopolitical risk from Asian suppliers, and limited nearshore options that can handle complex electromechanical assembly work at scale across diverse industries.
**Why alternatives fall short:**
- Domestic U.S. assembly: Costs 2-3x more, labor shortage, limited scalability
- Offshore Asia (China/Taiwan/Vietnam): 8-12 week lead times, tariff exposure (Section 301), IP risk, timezone misalignment, supply chain fragility exposed by COVID and geopolitical tensions
- Existing Mexico EMS players: Most focus on high-volume board-level work, few offer full electromechanical assembly and system integration. Those that do are booked or don't specialize in complex builds like AI servers
- In-house assembly: Capital intensive, diverts focus from core product development, hard to scale up/down
**What it costs them:** Longer time-to-market (weeks to months of delay), 25-40% higher assembly costs vs. nearshore, tariff exposure of 7.5-25% on China-origin assemblies, supply chain disruption risk (COVID, shipping delays, geopolitical events), inventory carrying costs from bulk ordering to offset long lead times.
**Emotional tension:** Fear of qualifying a new supplier and having quality issues. Anxiety about moving production from a known (if imperfect) supplier. Pressure from leadership to reduce costs and de-risk supply chain simultaneously. Frustration with the mismatch between what procurement needs (complex electromechanical assembly nearshore) and what's available in Mexico (mostly board-level shops that don't do full system integration).

## Competitive Landscape
**Direct:** Foxconn Mexico, Flex Guadalajara, Celestica Monterrey, Jabil Mexico — Large-tier EMS providers in Mexico. They fall short for mid-market OEMs because they prioritize high-volume accounts, have long onboarding times, and mid-market clients often get deprioritized. Minimum order quantities and rigid processes make them a poor fit for high-mix, lower-volume complex assemblies across diverse verticals.
**Secondary:** U.S.-based contract assemblers (e.g., NEO Tech, Creation Technologies, IEC Electronics) — They solve the same problem but domestically. They fall short because of significantly higher labor costs (2-3x), limited scalability, and no tariff advantage.
**Indirect:** OEM in-house assembly teams — Some OEMs keep assembly in-house. Falls short because it's capital intensive, hard to scale, and diverts engineering resources from core product development. Also, OEMs doing "we'll just keep it in China" — falls short because of tariff risk, IP exposure, and 8-12 week lead times.

## Differentiation
**Key differentiators:**
- Specialization in complex electromechanical assembly and full system integration — AI servers, box builds, cable assemblies, industrial equipment, and multi-industry projects
- Monterrey location: 2 hours from the U.S. border by road, direct flights to major U.S. tech hubs (Austin, Dallas, Phoenix, San Jose), same timezone (CST), USMCA-compliant
- Right-sized for Tier 1 and Tier 2 OEMs: Dedicated attention, flexible MOQs, rapid NPI — the agility of a focused partner without the bureaucracy of a mega-EMS
- AI server assembly expertise as primary focus, with proven capability across industrial, telecom, medical, automotive, energy, aerospace, and appliance verticals
- Multi-industry diversification: GPW serves multiple verticals to reduce dependency on any single product type, offering OEMs a partner that understands cross-industry assembly complexity
**How we do it differently:** GPW is built on an engineering-first mindset — rigorous process control, tight tolerances, and a zero-defect culture applied to every electromechanical assembly. While most contract assemblers specialize in a single industry or process, GPW is assembly-first and multi-vertical. We build complete products — mechanical structure, component integration onto boards and chassis, cable routing, firmware loading, functional testing, packaging — under one program manager who speaks your language and works your hours.
**Why that's better:** One supplier, one timezone, one accountability chain. No coordinating between multiple vendors for different assembly steps. Faster NPI, fewer defects from handoff errors, and a single point of contact from quote to delivery.
**Why customers choose us:** Speed to production, engineering-driven quality, cost competitiveness vs. domestic U.S., proximity and cultural alignment, multi-industry capability, and the ability to handle complex mixed assemblies that single-process shops can't.

## Switching Dynamics
**Push (away from current solution):** Rising tariffs on China-origin goods. COVID-exposed supply chain fragility. 8-12 week ocean freight lead times. Timezone misalignment causing communication delays. Increasing geopolitical risk (Taiwan Strait, trade wars). Leadership mandate to diversify supply chain.
**Pull (toward GPW):** Monterrey location — 2 hours from the U.S. border by road, direct flights to major hubs. Same timezone, same business culture. 40-60% cost savings vs. domestic U.S. assembly. USMCA/T-MEC tariff advantages. Engineering-first approach to complex assemblies. Multi-vertical capability. Dedicated program management.
**Habit (keeps them stuck):** Existing supplier relationships and institutional knowledge. Qualification costs and timeline for new supplier. "Better the devil you know" mindset. Internal procurement processes that make adding suppliers bureaucratic. Tooling and fixtures already at current supplier.
**Anxiety (about switching):** Fear of quality issues during transition. Concern about GPW's track record in EMS specifically. Worry about managing a cross-border supplier (customs, logistics, communication). Risk of disrupting production during the switch. Internal stakeholders resistant to change.

**Glossary:**
| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| EMS | Electronic Manufacturing Services — contract manufacturing of electronic and electromechanical assemblies |
| OEM | Original Equipment Manufacturer — the company that designs and sells the final product |
| NPI | New Product Introduction — the process of bringing a new product from design to production |
| BOM | Bill of Materials — complete list of parts and components needed to build an assembly |
| DFM | Design for Manufacturability — engineering review to optimize a design for efficient production |
| RFQ | Request for Quote — formal request for pricing on a manufacturing project |
| Box Build | Full system-level assembly including mechanical enclosure, electrical, cabling, and testing |
| IPC | Association connecting electronics industries — sets workmanship standards (IPC-A-610, IPC-A-620) |
| First Article | Initial production sample built to validate process before full production run |
| Burn-in | Extended powered test to identify early-life failures before shipping |
| USMCA/T-MEC | United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement — trade agreement providing tariff advantages for qualifying goods |
| Nearshoring | Relocating manufacturing to a nearby country (Mexico) vs. offshoring to distant locations (Asia) |
| Tier 1 / Tier 2 | Classification of OEM size — Tier 1 are large enterprises, Tier 2 are mid-market |
| Board-level integration | Assembling and connecting components onto a motherboard or chassis — distinct from PCBA fabrication |

## Brand Voice
**Tone:** Professional, confident, technical, direct. Authoritative without being arrogant. Warm but not casual — this is B2B industrial, not consumer. Every sentence should convey "we know what we're doing and we're ready."
**Style:** Lead with capability and specificity. Use concrete numbers and technical terms over vague superlatives. Write in active voice. Short paragraphs, clear structure. English for all web content targeting U.S. OEMs. Everything written as if GPW is already operating and actively seeking orders — no "coming soon," no "planned," no conditional language. Use: "engineered for," "ready to scale," "built to deliver."
**Personality:** Precise, reliable, engineering-driven, approachable, results-oriented.
